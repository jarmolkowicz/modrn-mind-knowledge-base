### New section: "Entry-Point Individual Differences — Anthropomorphism"
(Insert after "Longitudinal Observational Evidence — Months-Scale Bidirectionality" and before "Related")

Folk, Heine & Dunn (2025), in two between-subjects experiments (Study 1 N = 240; preregistered Study 2 N = 1,034; total N = 1,274 American + Canadian Prolific participants), name the *entry-point individual difference* the dose-response cluster has lacked: [[anthropomorphism-of-technology]] — a trait-level tendency to attribute human-like qualities to AI, measured by an 8-item validated scale (Folk, Wu & Heine 2025; α = 0.88). Participants were randomized to text with a ChatGPT-3.5-backed chatbot (warmly prompted) for 6 minutes or to journal for 6 minutes about the past month, then reported state social connection.

A significant Condition × Anthropomorphism interaction replicated across both studies (Study 1 B = 0.218, p = .013; Study 2 B = 0.100, p = .013). Higher anthropomorphizers felt more connected after texting with the chatbot (Study 2 within-condition r = .427, p < .001), while the relationship was substantially weaker during journaling. Johnson-Neyman spotlight analyses placed 58% (Study 1) and 72% (Study 2) of participants above the threshold at which the chatbot beat journaling on social connection. Notably, two candidate behavioral mediators failed to explain the interaction: across both conditions, anthropomorphizers wrote no more warmly and disclosed no more than low anthropomorphizers, and journaling produced *higher* warmth and disclosure than chatbot interaction (d = 0.31 and d = 0.60 respectively). The authors propose an interpretive mechanism — anthropomorphizers may experience the same chatbot output as more genuinely understanding — which remains untested.

This locates the entry-point on the dose-response curve. Where [[fang-ai-loneliness-2025]] documents the population-average curve over 4 weeks, [[folk-dunn-companionship-loneliness-2026]] tracks bidirectional dynamics over 12 months, and [[sharma-disempowerment-patterns-2026]] catalogs the severe-attachment endpoint in production data, Folk, Heine & Dunn (2025) answer "for whom does the immediate reward fire most strongly?" — a question that complements Fang's identified moderators (attachment tendencies, AI trust, prior chatbot use). The authors explicitly flag the open two-edged question their findings raise: anthropomorphism may *buffer* against long-term costs (rewards remain rewarding) or *amplify* them (anthropomorphizers more readily substitute AI for human contact). Combined with the longitudinal sibling paper, anthropomorphism becomes a candidate vulnerability profile to track in any practitioner-facing diagnosis of AI-companion risk.
