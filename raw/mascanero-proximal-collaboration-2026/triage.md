## Summary

Mascareño, Wörtler, Przegalińska & Ciechanowski (2026, *Computers in Human Behavior Reports* 22:101054). Experimental between-subjects study (N=221 employees) testing how *proximal* human-AI collaboration (close, sustained interaction with AI during collaborative tasks) affects two innovation-process stages that prior research has neglected: **idea selection** and **idea implementation**.

Theoretical anchor: Distributed Cognition theory (Hutchins 1995). Distributing cognition and decision-making across human + AI agents weakens individual accountability and diminishes attentional focus on execution.

Findings:
- Proximal human-AI collaboration **reduced idea selection**, particularly when idea originality was low.
- Proximal collaboration **reduced idea implementation**, conditional on reliance on AI — the negative effect weakened as reliance increased.
- Idea originality moderates the selection effect; reliance on AI moderates the implementation effect.

The paper's contribution is to push beyond the literature's focus on creativity (idea *generation*) into the downstream stages where AI's effects are paradoxical: more proximity often equals worse selection and worse implementation, opposite to the "AI as innovation accelerator" narrative.
