## Summary

Messeri (anthropology, Yale) and Crockett (psychology and human values, Princeton) develop a framework for the epistemic risks of AI in scientific research. They argue that scientists' enthusiasm for AI tools comes from promises to overcome cognitive limitations and human bias, but these same features make researchers vulnerable to **illusions of understanding** — metacognitive errors in which people believe they understand more than they actually do. The paper is a Perspective (peer-reviewed argumentation, not new empirical data), published in Nature, drawing on cognitive science, social epistemology, philosophy of science, and STS literatures.

The argument is built on a four-part taxonomy of AI visions across the research pipeline: **Oracle** (literature search/hypothesis generation), **Surrogate** (synthetic data, including AI participants in social science), **Quant** (analysis of large/complex data), and **Arbiter** (peer review and replicability prediction). Each vision is appealing because it promises both productivity (more output, less cost) and objectivity (less bias, no standpoint). The paper introduces three named illusions: **illusion of explanatory depth** (importing an existing concept from Rozenblit & Keil 2002), **illusion of exploratory breadth** (paper-original; the false belief that AI is exploring all testable hypotheses when it explores only those AI can test), and **illusion of objectivity** (paper-original; the false belief that AI tools have no standpoint or represent all standpoints).

The synthesis-level construct is **scientific monocultures** — situations in which one approach to knowledge production crowds out alternatives, with two sub-variants: **monocultures of knowing** (one set of methods/questions dominates because it fits AI) and **monocultures of knowers** (one standpoint dominates because AI embeds the values of its training data and developers). This connects directly to KB clusters around AI-induced confidence inflation, fluency-driven trust, and homogenization of knowledge production.
