## Proposed Additions

### To "Key Insight" or a new subsection on metacognitive sensitivity under AI

Fernandes et al. (2026) provide direct empirical evidence that metacognitive *sensitivity* — not just bias — is degraded in human–AI interaction. In two large studies on logical-reasoning tasks (Study 1 N = 246; Study 2 N = 452 randomized), participants' mean AUC (a measure of how well their confidence ratings distinguished their correct from incorrect answers) fell to ~.62 — significantly below the conventional .70 acceptable benchmark, for both AI and no-AI groups. People felt confident regardless of whether they were right.

This adds a sensitivity dimension to the KB's existing treatment of metacognitive failure under AI. The metacognitive monitoring problem is not only that confidence is inflated globally (`[[confidence-competence-gap]]`) — it is also that confidence ratings, trial by trial, no longer track correctness reliably. The "Did I evaluate this AI output?" question depends on a metacognitive signal that is increasingly noisy.

A monetary incentive for accurate metacognition (+£0.50 in Study 2) did not improve calibration relative to Study 1, suggesting low effort is not the primary driver. Authors interpret this through Tankelevitch et al.'s (2024) framework: high-quality AI assistance may overshadow the internal cues people normally use to monitor their own knowledge (Koriat, 1997).

The paper also confirms that the Dunning–Kruger pattern of skill-scaled miscalibration disappears under AI use — see `[[confidence-competence-gap]]` for the Bayesian bias/noise decomposition. This is a metacognitive-monitoring story: under AI, the cues that normally inform low performers about their own miscalibration are masked by uniformly fluent AI outputs.
