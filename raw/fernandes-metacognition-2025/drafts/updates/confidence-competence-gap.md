## Proposed Additions

### To "Why It Matters" or a new dedicated subsection

Fernandes et al. (2026) provide the first computational-model decomposition of the confidence-competence gap under AI use, plus a counterintuitive finding about AI literacy. In two studies (N = 246 + 452, LSAT logical-reasoning items), they show:

- **Quantification**: AI use improved task performance by ~3 points (out of 20) but inflated self-estimates by ~4 points — a robust ~1-point overconfidence gap that *doubled* the metacognitive bias of the no-AI Jansen et al. (2021) benchmark.
- **Bayesian bias/noise decomposition**: A computational model separates bias (*b_k* > 0 = uniform overestimation) from noise (*σ_k* > 1 = skill-scaled miscalibration). Under AI use, the noise parameter collapses to ~1 (95% HDI [0.84, 1.19]), eliminating the skill-scaling that normally produces the Dunning–Kruger pattern. Under no-AI conditions, *σ* = 1.78, sustaining the classic DKE gradient.
- **DKE flattening**: AI does not correct overconfidence for low performers — it raises them to a uniform high baseline so that everyone overestimates roughly equally. Authors call this the "augmentation hypothesis": AI compresses skill-based variance in both performance and self-assessment.
- **AI literacy paradox**: Higher self-rated AI literacy correlated with *lower* metacognitive accuracy across both studies (overall SNAIL × overestimation: *r* = .21 in Study 1, *r* = .20 in Study 2; both *p* < .01). The Technical Understanding subscale (familiarity with prompting, parameters, API workflows) carried the strongest effect — suggesting that procedural AI fluency inflates confidence without improving discrimination, an instance of the illusion of explanatory depth (Fisher & Oppenheimer, 2021). This challenges the assumption that AI-literacy training is uniformly protective against the gap.
- **Incentives don't close it**: A monetary metacognitive-accuracy bonus (+£0.50, ~8% of compensation) in Study 2 did not reduce overestimation — effort is not the bottleneck.
- **Sensitivity is uniformly low**: Mean AUC (confidence-correctness discrimination) was ~.62 for both AI and no-AI groups, significantly below the .70 acceptable threshold. People felt confident, but their confidence ratings did not reliably distinguish their correct from incorrect answers.
