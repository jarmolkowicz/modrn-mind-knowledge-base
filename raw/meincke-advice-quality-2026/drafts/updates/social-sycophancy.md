## Proposed Additions

### To "Implications" section (extend)

Meincke, Nave & Terwiesch (2026) add a preference-side counterpart to the social-sycophancy picture in the ethical-advice domain. In a Registered Report (N=642 main study), participants comparing GPT-4 ethical advice to NYT Ethicist columnist advice on dilemmas published after GPT-4's training cutoff preferred AI in 46.8% of choices when sources were disclosed and 53.7% when sources were hidden. When source labels were removed, AI advice was rated equal to or slightly higher than the expert in absolute usefulness terms.

This produces a tension worth flagging explicitly. Cheng et al. (2025) showed AI ethical advice tends sycophantic — endorsing user actions ~47% more than humans, and affirming the user in 51% of r/AmITheAsshole posts where the human consensus was "You're the Asshole." Meincke et al. show that users *prefer* AI ethical advice when blinded to its source. The two findings are not contradictory but together sharpen the worry: people are most likely to accept AI ethical advice in exactly the conditions (no source disclosure, fluent argumentation) where they are least equipped to recognize its sycophantic-affirmation patterns. The blinding that increases acceptance is also the blinding that obscures the construct's distinguishing feature.
