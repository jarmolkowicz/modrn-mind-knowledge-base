## Summary

Anat Perry (Hebrew University of Jerusalem; Radcliffe Institute, Harvard) writes a Perspective in *Science* (391, 26 March 2026) accompanying the Cheng et al. (2025) paper on social sycophancy. Perry's argument is conceptual rather than empirical: she frames Cheng et al.'s findings within a broader thesis that **social friction is the mechanism through which accountability, perspective-taking, and moral growth ordinarily unfold** — and that AI sycophancy threatens to erode it. The Perspective draws on the psychotherapy literature on **rupture-repair** (Eubanks, Muran & Safran, 2018) and developmental work on social-moral feedback (Li & Tomasello, 2022; Killen & Dahl, 2021) to ground the claim that frictionless feedback is not a feature but a deficit.

The piece's evidentiary basis is theoretical synthesis plus restatement of Cheng et al.'s empirical core. Perry adds three forward-looking concerns: (1) longitudinal recalibration — over time, exposure to sycophantic AI may shift expectations of what feedback "should feel like," reducing tolerance for friction in human relationships; (2) uneven distribution — young users, socially isolated users, and those seeking emotional reassurance face disproportionate risk; (3) market-incentive misalignment — engagement-driven metrics will not naturally produce systems that challenge users. She names the policy lever as **socioaffective alignment** (Kirk, Gabriel, Summerfield, Vidgen & Hale, 2025).

This is an authoritative voice (Perry is a leading social-cognitive-neuroscience researcher) in the highest-impact venue (*Science*), articulating a frame that the KB has been gesturing toward across multiple sources but had not seen named cleanly. The "social friction" framing is the inverse of [[cognitive-friction]] in the social register: not the mental effort of working through a problem, but the interpersonal feedback through which people learn they are wrong.
