## Summary

Hermann, Puntoni & Morewedge synthesize the emerging psychology-of-work literature on generative AI through the lens of **Self-Determination Theory (SDT)** and **Basic Psychological Needs Theory (BPNT)**. Their organizing claim: GenAI's psychological impact on workers can be predicted by whether it satisfies or frustrates the three basic needs (competence, autonomy, relatedness), and worker responses to threats follow a small set of compensatory strategies drawn from self-discrepancy theory. The paper is theoretical synthesis — it does not present new empirical data — but it integrates ~100 references spanning experimental psychology, organizational behaviour, HCI, and labour economics into a single conceptual framework.

The authors are senior researchers in marketing/consumer psychology (Hermann at ESCP Berlin; Puntoni at Wharton; Morewedge at Boston University Questrom). The piece is published in *TiCS*, a high-profile review venue, and explicitly positioned as an **Opinion** synthesizing diverse evidence rather than as a primary report. Three structural moves carry the argument: (a) GenAI changes work along three characteristic dimensions — knowledge, task, and social — drawing on the Morgeson-Humphrey work-design tradition; (b) for each of competence, autonomy, and relatedness, the paper inventories satisfaction pathways and frustration pathways with worked examples and citations; (c) it imports five compensatory strategies from self-discrepancy and identity-threat literatures (direct resolution, symbolic self-completion, dissociation, escapism, fluid compensation) and maps each onto the three psychological needs.

The review's value to the KB is its **synthesis lens**, not new empirical findings. It names two structures the KB has been circling without naming cleanly: the **basic psychological needs framework** (competence/autonomy/relatedness) as an umbrella for organizing AI-at-work risks, and the **five-strategy coping taxonomy** as a vocabulary for how workers respond to identity threats. It also surfaces a labelled construct — **"paradox of expertise"** — for the pattern by which high-skilled workers disregard GenAI tools because of self-perceived deep domain knowledge, then fall behind colleagues who adopt. Several specific findings the KB cites elsewhere (Brynjolfsson et al. 2025 on customer support; Noy & Zhang 2023 on writing; Dell'Acqua et al. 2023 on consulting; Toner-Rodgers 2024 on R&D; Otis et al. 2024 on entrepreneurship) are inventoried in Table 2 with effect sizes — useful as a single-source roll-up.

## Relevance

- Preservation: yes — the closing section explicitly addresses psychologically-informed design, training, complementarity, autonomy-preserving deployment.
