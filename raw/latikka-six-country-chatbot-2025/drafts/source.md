# Latikka et al. (2026) — Individual and Well-being Factors in Social Chatbot Usage: A Six-Country Study

## Citation

Latikka, R., Bergdahl, J., Savolainen, I., Celuch, M., & Oksanen, A. (2026). Individual and well-being factors associated with social chatbot usage: A six-country study. *Journal of Social and Personal Relationships*, 43(4), 1055–1079.

**DOI:** [10.1177/02654075251392956](https://doi.org/10.1177/02654075251392956)

(Tampere University, Finland, with Demos Helsinki. Cross-sectional cross-national survey study; second wave of the longitudinal Self & Technology project. Funded by Tampere University internal funding 2022–2024 and Kone Foundation grant 202011325; PI: Atte Oksanen.)

## Type

Paper (cross-sectional cross-national survey; logistic + robust linear regression; N = 5,663 across six European countries)

## Key Insight

A six-country survey of European adults (Finland, France, Germany, Ireland, Italy, Poland; total N = 5,663) finds two cross-culturally robust associations with social chatbot ("chatbot friend") use: users are younger than nonusers in every country (p < .001 throughout), and chatbot use is positively associated with psychological distress in every country (full-model p ranging from .044 to <.001). Loneliness is associated with chatbot use in 4/6 countries (France, Germany, Italy, Poland) but not Finland or Ireland. Self-esteem reaches significance only in France (positive). Frequency of face-to-face social contact is *not* associated with chatbot use in any country — challenging a simple "displacement" framing on the objective social-contact dimension.

## Key Findings

**Sample and design**
- N = 5,663 total: Finland (1,095), France (1,014), Germany (900), Ireland (588), Italy (1,099), Poland (967).
- Adults aged 18–75 from Norstat online panels; second wave of the Self & Technology longitudinal study, collected October–November 2023.
- Wave-1 → Wave-2 retention: 71% (Finland), 65% (France), 58.9% (Germany), 52.8% (Ireland), 71.8% (Italy), 63.1% (Poland).
- Surveys in each country's most commonly spoken official language. Sample broadly aligned with Eurostat age/gender distributions.

**Measures**
- Social chatbot use: single item "How often do you use a chatbot friend (e.g., Replica, My AI)?", 0 = I do not use to 4 = many times a day; collapsed to a binary user/non-user dummy ([p.8]).
- Loneliness: 3-item R-UCLA-3 (Hughes et al. 2004); ω = 0.83–0.87 across countries.
- Psychological distress: 5-item Mental Health Inventory (MHI-5; Berwick et al. 1991); ω = 0.82–0.87.
- Self-esteem: single item "I have high self-esteem" (Robins et al. 2001), 1–7.
- Face-to-face social contact: ESS adapted item, 0 (never) to 6 (every day).
- General positive attitude toward new technologies: single item, 1–7.
- Sociodemographics: age, gender, working status, monthly gross income (8-band), relationship status, parental status.

**Country-level prevalence of social chatbot use ([p.9–10])**
- Italy 17.93% (highest) | France 14.69% | Poland 12.72% | Finland 12.24% | Germany 9.67% | Ireland 8.67% (lowest).
- Prevalence does not track digital-skills ranking (Finland and Ireland have highest digital skills but mid-to-low chatbot use; Italy has below-EU-average digital skills but highest chatbot use).

**Logistic regression — predictors of any chatbot use ([p.11], Table 2)**
- **Younger age** in all six countries (OR = 0.94–0.97; p < .001 throughout).
- **Male gender** in Germany (p = .012), Ireland (p = .017), Poland (p = .024); n.s. elsewhere.
- **Being a parent** in Ireland (p = .016) and Italy (p < .001); n.s. elsewhere.
- **Higher income** in France (p = .011) and Italy (p = .014).
- **Stronger general positive technology attitude** in Finland, France, Italy, Poland (all p ≤ .025); n.s. in Germany and Ireland.
- Frequency of face-to-face social contact: **n.s. in every country**.

**Robust regression — chatbot use as predictor of psychological distress ([p.13–14], Table 3)**
- 0-model (chatbot use only): significant in all six countries (p ≤ .001).
- Full model (controlling for age, gender, marital status, parental status, income, work, social contact):
  - Finland B = 0.87, p = .044
  - France B = 1.29, p = .003
  - Germany B = 2.09, p < .001
  - Ireland B = 2.31, p = .002
  - Italy B = 0.97, p = .006
  - Poland B = 1.45, p = .001
- Direction is the same in every country: chatbot users report higher psychological distress.

**Robust regression — chatbot use as predictor of loneliness ([p.15], Table 4)**
- 0-model: significant in all six countries.
- Full model:
  - **Significant**: France (B = 0.59, p = .002), Germany (B = 0.70, p = .019), Italy (B = 0.49, p = .002), Poland (B = 0.50, p = .013).
  - **Not significant**: Finland (B = 0.08, p = .661), Ireland (B = 0.55, p = .105).

**Robust regression — chatbot use as predictor of self-esteem ([p.16], Table 5)**
- Full-model significance only in France (B = 0.34, p = .016, *positive*).
- Five other countries: n.s. (Finland p = .084, Germany p = .231, Ireland p = .427, Italy p = .196, Poland p = .455).

**Cross-cultural reading**
- Most consistent findings: younger age + higher psychological distress, in every country.
- Variable findings (loneliness, self-esteem, gender, parenthood, income, technology attitude) suggest country-level moderators in cultural and digital context that this study does not unpack.
- Authors explicitly caution that mechanisms behind country-level differences require deeper national-context examination using qualitative and mixed methods ([p.17]).

## Causal-Inference Caveats (Authors' Own)

- Cross-sectional design — directionality cannot be established. Authors acknowledge that psychologically stressed people may be more likely to seek out social chatbots (selection) rather than (or in addition to) chatbot use causing distress.
- Single-item measures for self-esteem and chatbot use; future work should use longer scales and graded usage measures.
- Online-panel recruitment may over-represent technology-engaged respondents, biasing prevalence estimates.
- Chatbot use was binary (any vs. none); does not distinguish low from high usage, motivations, or chatbot type.
- Six robustness signals (consistent direction across countries with different cultures, digital skills, languages, response rates) reduce but do not eliminate the threat of unmeasured confounders.

## Relevance

Three load-bearing contributions:

- **Adds the cross-cultural breadth the [[ai-loneliness-effect]] cluster has lacked.** [[fang-ai-loneliness-2025]] is US-leaning (MIT/OpenAI sample); [[folk-dunn-companionship-loneliness-2026]] is UK/US/Canada/Australia (Anglophone Western); [[folk-heine-dunn-anthropomorphism-2025]] is US/Canada Prolific. Latikka et al.'s six EU countries — including East-European Poland and South-European Italy alongside Nordic and West-European samples — is the first study in the cluster to test generalizability across substantially different cultural and digital-skill contexts. The age + distress associations replicate everywhere; loneliness association is more variable.
- **Refines the displacement framing.** Frequency of face-to-face social contact does *not* predict chatbot use in any country. The association lives in subjective loneliness and distress, not in objective social-contact volume. This is consistent with the substitution arm of [[mira-model]] principle 4 in a particular way: users with adequate human contact who nonetheless feel lonely or distressed are who turn to chatbots, not users objectively isolated from others.
- **Country-level variation as a research signal.** Loneliness × chatbot association is significant in France, Germany, Italy, Poland, but not Finland or Ireland; self-esteem only in France; technology attitude in 4/6 countries. The variation itself is a finding — practitioners deploying chatbot interventions across populations cannot assume uniform user profiles or uniform well-being correlates. Authors flag national-context examination as necessary follow-up.

## Supports

- [[ai-loneliness-effect]] — adds six-country observational evidence for the dose-response cluster; replicates the higher-distress association across all six samples and the loneliness association in 4/6 samples; strengthens the cross-cultural generalizability claim.
- [[anthropomorphism-of-technology]] — sibling paper [[folk-heine-dunn-anthropomorphism-2025]] flagged cultural variation in attitudes toward social chatbots as an open question; Latikka et al.'s country-level prevalence and well-being-correlate variation supplies preliminary cross-cultural data, though without measuring anthropomorphism directly.
- [[mira-model]] — empirical evidence on principle 4 (relational substitution vs. enhancement). The objective-vs-subjective distinction (face-to-face contact n.s.; subjective loneliness/distress significant) sharpens MIRA's substitution mechanism toward perceived rather than enacted social deficit.
- [[capacity-erosion]] — younger users disproportionately represented among chatbot users in every country aligns with the population-distribution concerns articulated in the loneliness cluster.

## Contradicts / Extends

- **Extends** [[fang-ai-loneliness-2025]]'s individual-vulnerability findings to a cross-cultural population. Where Fang documents dose-dependent within-experiment effects, Latikka et al. document cross-sectional between-person differences across six national contexts.
- **Extends** [[folk-dunn-companionship-loneliness-2026]] geographically — adds continental-Europe evidence to that paper's UK/US/Canada/Australia sample. Direction-consistent: users tend toward higher loneliness, not lower (in 4/6 countries).
- **Refines** [[folk-heine-dunn-anthropomorphism-2025]]'s open generalizability question. Latikka et al. do not measure anthropomorphism, but supply six-country variation in chatbot uptake and well-being correlates that any future cross-cultural test of anthropomorphism's moderating role should account for.
- **Complicates** simple "AI displaces human contact" narratives. Frequency of face-to-face social contact is uncorrelated with chatbot use across all six countries — chatbot use lives in the subjective-loneliness/distress register, not the objective-isolation register.
- Consistent with [[boyd-markowitz-human-connection-2026]]'s framing that AI relational use is moderated by user disposition (here: age, distress, country-specific factors).

## Open Questions

- **Causal direction.** Cross-sectional design makes selection (distressed users seek chatbots) and causation (chatbot use elevates distress) observationally equivalent. Authors recommend longitudinal and experimental designs.
- **Country-level mechanisms.** Why is the loneliness association significant in France, Germany, Italy, and Poland, but not Finland or Ireland? Why is self-esteem only positive in France? The authors flag this as requiring qualitative and mixed-methods national-context work — beyond the scope of the current study.
- **Granularity of usage.** Binary user/non-user collapses very different usage patterns. The Folk & Dunn (2026) sample showed ~70–74% never-users and very low daily use; Latikka et al.'s binary measure cannot distinguish casual from heavy use, leaving the dose-response curve unaddressed.
- **Anthropomorphism of technology cross-culturally.** Folk, Heine & Dunn (2025) named the entry-point individual-difference moderator but flagged cross-cultural generalizability as open. Latikka et al. do not measure it, leaving the moderator untested across European cultural contexts.
- **Chatbot type.** "Chatbot friend (e.g., Replika, My AI)" collapses general-purpose AI assistants and dedicated companion apps. The mix likely differs across countries and may explain part of the country-level variation.
- **Self-selection in panel recruitment.** Norstat panel respondents may over-represent technology-engaged adults, potentially inflating prevalence and dampening associations with technology attitude.
