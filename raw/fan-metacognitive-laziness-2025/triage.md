## Summary

Fan, Tang, Le, Shen, Tan, Zhao, Shen, Li & Gašević (2025, *British Journal of Educational Technology* 56(2):489-530, DOI: 10.1111/bjet.13544; preprint accepted Nov 2024) report a 4-arm randomised lab experiment (N=117 university students) comparing learning with **ChatGPT vs. human expert vs. writing-analytics checklist vs. no extra tool** on a writing task. Multi-channel data captured intrinsic motivation (IMI), self-regulated learning (SRL) processes, essay-score improvement, knowledge gain, and knowledge transfer.

Three findings:
1. **No significant differences in intrinsic motivation across groups.**
2. **Significant differences in SRL processes** — particularly during revision: ChatGPT/HE/CL groups all showed more elaboration/organisation/orientation than control. But the AI group's SRL processes were tightly tied to ChatGPT interactions and showed *fewer* metacognitive transitions (e.g., between orientation and evaluation) than the human-expert group.
3. **AI group outperformed all others on essay-score improvement** (~2 points higher than each comparison, all p<0.05) but showed **no difference in knowledge gain or knowledge transfer** vs. other groups.

The paper formally defines **metacognitive laziness**: "learners' dependence on AI assistance, offloading metacognitive load and less effectively associating responsible metacognitive processes with learning tasks." This is positioned as an extension of Risko & Gilbert's (2016) cognitive offloading from informational tasks to metacognitive ones. The authors warn that AI may produce short-term performance gains via "AI-empowered learning skills" at the expense of long-term skill development.
