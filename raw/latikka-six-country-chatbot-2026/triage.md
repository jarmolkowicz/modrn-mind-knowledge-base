## Summary

A cross-sectional, cross-national survey study of social-chatbot ("chatbot friend") use across six European countries — Finland (N=1,095), France (N=1,014), Germany (N=900), Ireland (N=588), Italy (N=1,099), and Poland (N=967) — collected in autumn 2023 as the second wave of the longitudinal Self & Technology study. Adults aged 18–75 were sampled through Norstat online panels, with response rates from the first wave between ~53% and 72%. The team estimated logistic and linear (robust) regressions to ask: (a) which individual characteristics predict who uses a social chatbot at all, and (b) how does social chatbot usage relate to psychological distress, loneliness, and self-esteem.

Empirical evidence, observational, cross-sectional. Authors are at Tampere University (Finland) and Demos Helsinki — a research group with prior cross-national work on technology use, well-being, and online relationships. The study uses validated short-form scales (R-UCLA-3 loneliness, MHI-5 distress, single-item self-esteem, ESS social-contact item) and a binary user/non-user chatbot-use measure. Country prevalence of social chatbot use ranged from 8.67% (Ireland) to 17.93% (Italy). Across countries, two associations were robust: (1) social chatbot users were younger than nonusers in all six countries (p < .001 throughout); (2) social chatbot use was positively associated with psychological distress in all six countries (full-model p ranging from .044 to <.001). Loneliness × chatbot-use was significant in France, Germany, Italy, and Poland, but not in Finland or Ireland. Self-esteem × chatbot-use reached significance only in France (positive). Frequency of face-to-face social contact was *not* associated with chatbot use in any country.

The contribution is the cross-national breadth and the test of generalizability rather than the discovery of new constructs. The paper's framing leans on existing theory (Computers Are Social Actors, Media Are Social Actors, the Media Evocation paradigm; Baumeister & Leary's need to belong; Granovetter's strong/weak ties) and existing constructs (loneliness, psychological distress, self-esteem). Its empirical news is what holds across very different cultural and digital-skill contexts and what varies. The authors are explicit about being cross-sectional and unable to infer causality.

## Relevance
- Risk: yes — psychological-distress association across all six countries adds to evidence that social chatbot use may either index or contribute to mental-health vulnerability; younger-user pattern raises population-distribution concerns.
- Erosion: yes — loneliness association in 4/6 countries, alongside the cross-cultural framing, is direction-consistent with [[ai-loneliness-effect]]'s dose-response cluster.
- Preservation: indirect — by mapping who is using social chatbots and where the well-being correlates appear, the study informs practitioners' diagnosis but offers no prescriptive practice.
- **Relevance**: HIGH

## Quality
- Evidence basis: large total N (5,663), six culturally and digitally divergent EU countries, validated short scales, robust regression for heteroskedasticity, conventional logistic + linear regression frame. Cross-sectional design (acknowledged by authors); single-item self-esteem and binary chatbot-use measure are weaker than ideal. Online-panel recruitment likely over-represents tech-engaged respondents.
- Originality: the cross-national replication is the originality — six EU countries is rare in this literature. Compared against [[fang-ai-loneliness-2025]] (4-week RCT, US-leaning sample), [[folk-dunn-companionship-loneliness-2026]] (12-month longitudinal, UK/US/Canada/Australia), [[folk-heine-dunn-anthropomorphism-2025]] (US/Canada Prolific experiments), and [[sharma-disempowerment-patterns-2026]] (production data), Latikka complements all four by adding the European cross-cultural breadth that the existing cluster lacks.
- **Quality**: HIGH

- Claims:
  - Younger age predicts social chatbot use across six countries (universal).
  - Higher psychological distress is associated with social chatbot use across all six countries (universal).
  - Higher loneliness is associated with social chatbot use in 4/6 countries (France, Germany, Italy, Poland) but not Finland or Ireland (variable).
  - Higher self-esteem is associated with social chatbot use only in France.
  - Frequency of face-to-face social contact is *not* associated with chatbot use in any country — challenges the displacement/compensation framing on the objective social-contact dimension.
  - Country-level prevalence ranges from ~9% (Ireland, Germany) to ~18% (Italy).
  - Stronger general positive attitude toward new technologies predicts chatbot use in Finland, France, Italy, and Poland — but not in Germany or Ireland.
