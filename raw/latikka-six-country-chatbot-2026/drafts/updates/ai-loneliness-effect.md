**## Cross-Cultural Generalizability — Six European Countries**

Latikka et al. (2026), in *Journal of Social and Personal Relationships*, add the cross-cultural breadth the dose-response cluster has lacked. Cross-sectional surveys collected in autumn 2023 across six European countries — Finland (N=1,095), France (N=1,014), Germany (N=900), Ireland (N=588), Italy (N=1,099), Poland (N=967); total N = 5,663 — supply the first comparative test of social chatbot ("chatbot friend") use across substantially different cultural and digital-skill contexts. Where [[fang-ai-loneliness-2025]] is US-leaning, [[folk-dunn-companionship-loneliness-2026]] samples UK/US/Canada/Australia, and [[folk-heine-dunn-anthropomorphism-2025]] is US/Canada Prolific, Latikka et al. cover Nordic, West-European, South-European, and East-European samples within a single design.

Two associations replicate in every country: chatbot users are younger than nonusers (OR = 0.94–0.97; p < .001 throughout), and chatbot use is positively associated with psychological distress in the full model (B = 0.87 to 2.31; p ranging from .044 to <.001). The loneliness association is significant in France, Germany, Italy, and Poland (B = 0.49–0.70; p ≤ .019), but not in Finland (p = .661) or Ireland (p = .105). Self-esteem reaches significance only in France (B = 0.34, p = .016, positive). Country-level prevalence ranges from 8.67% (Ireland) to 17.93% (Italy) and does not track digital-skills ranking — Italy has below-EU-average digital skills but the highest chatbot uptake; Finland and Ireland have the highest digital skills but mid-to-low chatbot use.

The most theoretically consequential finding: frequency of face-to-face social contact is *not* associated with chatbot use in any of the six countries. The association lives in subjective loneliness and distress, not in objective social-contact volume. Users with adequate human contact who nonetheless feel lonely or distressed are who turn to chatbots — not users objectively isolated from others. This refines the substitution framing: the dose-response curve operates on the felt-deficit register, not the contact-frequency register.

The cross-sectional design means causal direction remains observationally equivalent — distressed/lonely users may seek chatbots, chatbots may elevate distress/loneliness, or both. But the cross-country replication of age + distress associations across six culturally and digitally divergent samples represents a strong cross-cultural generalizability signal at the population level. The variability in loneliness, self-esteem, gender, parenthood, income, and technology-attitude associations across countries is itself a finding: practitioners deploying chatbot interventions cannot assume uniform user profiles or uniform well-being correlates across populations.
