# Tri-System Theory

## Overview

Tri-System Theory extends Kahneman's dual-process framework to account for AI as a third cognitive system. It addresses a gap in traditional models: they assume all cognition occurs within the biological mind, which no longer holds when people routinely delegate reasoning to AI. The framework models how internal and external cognition interact, compete, and converge.

## Author

Steven D. Shaw & Gideon Nave, The Wharton School, University of Pennsylvania (2026)

## Core Idea

Modern decision-making unfolds within a triadic cognitive ecology, not a purely internal dual-process system. AI (System 3) is not merely a tool — it is a functional cognitive agent whose presence reshapes how and when humans engage their own faculties. The central question shifts from "Is this judgment fast or slow?" to "Whose cognition produced this judgment?"

## Key Components

- **System 1 (Fast)**: Internal, intuitive, heuristic-based. Fast, automatic, emotion-driven. Prone to bias but experientially grounded.
- **System 2 (Slow)**: Internal, deliberative, rule-based. Slow, effortful, analytical. Normative but costly.
- **System 3 (Artificial)**: External, automated, data-driven. Fast, scalable, emotion-neutral. High accuracy in structured domains; brittle in open-ended tasks. Lacks affect, situational judgment, and phenomenological understanding.

### Canonical Cognitive Routes

1. **Intuition**: Stimulus -> System 1 -> Response (no AI involvement)
2. **Deliberation**: Stimulus -> System 1 -> conflict detected -> System 2 -> Response
3. **Cognitive Offloading**: Stimulus -> System 1/2 -> System 3 (assist) -> System 1/2 -> Response (strategic delegation; System 2 remains active)
4. **Cognitive Surrender**: Stimulus -> System 1 (brief) -> System 3 -> Response (minimal internal engagement; uncritical adoption)
5. **Autopilot**: Stimulus -> System 3 -> Response (no internal engagement at all)
6. **Hybrid/Recursive**: Verify-then-adopt, override-then-rationalize, and other loopbacks

## Strengths

- Provides a structured vocabulary for how AI integrates into human cognition, moving beyond vague notions of "AI assistance"
- Distinguishes cognitive surrender from cognitive offloading — a practically important distinction for educators and practitioners
- Empirical support from 3 preregistered experiments (N=1,372; 9,593 trials) with large effect sizes (Cohen's h = 0.81), though all used a single task type (CRT) — domain-specific generalizability remains untested
- Identifies measurable individual-difference predictors (trust in AI, need for cognition, fluid intelligence)
- Generates actionable design implications (feedback + incentives as partial countermeasures)
- Explicitly positions itself as complementary to existing concepts (extended mind, automation bias, transactive memory) rather than replacing them

## Limitations

- All experiments used a single task type (Cognitive Reflection Test) — generalizability to professional judgment, creative work, moral reasoning, or everyday decisions is untested
- Lab-controlled single-session design — no evidence on how surrender dynamics evolve with repeated AI use over time
- System 3 is defined broadly (any external algorithmic cognition) but tested only with ChatGPT/GPT-4o — may not generalize to different AI interfaces, modalities, or reliability profiles
- The "system" metaphor inherits the well-known criticisms of dual-process theory itself (Melnikoff & Bargh, 2018; Keren & Schul, 2009) — whether these are truly distinct "systems" vs. points on a continuum remains debatable
- Does not address embodied cognition, emotional reasoning, or the role of domain expertise in moderating surrender
- Framing AI as a "cognitive agent" may overstate AI's role — AI has no goals, no understanding, and no stake in outcomes; calling it a "system" alongside biological cognition risks anthropomorphizing
- Preprint status (SSRN) — not yet peer-reviewed as of screening date
