# Identity-Threat Coping

## Overview

A descriptive taxonomy of five compensatory strategies workers use to cope with the psychological threats GenAI poses to their basic needs (competence, autonomy, relatedness). Imported by Hermann, Puntoni & Morewedge (2025) from self-discrepancy research and applied to the GenAI workplace context. Useful for diagnosing how workers are responding to AI integration and for designing interventions that channel them toward productive strategies rather than withdrawal.

## What It Is / How It Works

Hermann, Puntoni & Morewedge (2025) adapt five strategies from the self-discrepancy and identity-threat literatures:

1. **Direct resolution** — Behavior that resolves the source of the discrepancy. Examples: enrolling in skill-building training, adapting workflows to incorporate GenAI effectively, learning prompt engineering. Addresses the threat at its root but requires time, effort, and often organizational support.

2. **Symbolic self-completion** — Behavior that signals mastery in the domain of the discrepancy without resolving it. Examples: emphasizing credentials, titles, or human-only domain expertise in communications; foregrounding "what GenAI can't do that I can" in self-presentation. Cheap and fast; does not address the source of the threat but restores self-perception.

3. **Dissociation** — Behavior that separates oneself from the domain where the discrepancy is triggered. Examples: avoiding GenAI-dependent tasks, withdrawing psychologically from areas where GenAI exposes skill gaps, reducing identification with threatened parts of the role. Provides immediate relief at the cost of professional engagement.

4. **Escapism** — Behavior that distracts from thinking about the discrepancy. Examples: extended breaks, requesting reassignment, disengaging from work tasks, browsing unrelated content during work hours. The most maladaptive strategy in workplace contexts; correlates with emotional exhaustion and reduced performance.

5. **Fluid compensation** — Behavior that reinforces other, unthreatened aspects of identity. Examples: a marketer threatened by GenAI on content creation shifting identification toward campaign strategy; a developer threatened by code generation emphasizing system architecture or mentorship. Productive when the alternative domain is genuinely valuable; can be disguised dissociation if the shift is purely defensive.

The taxonomy maps onto the three basic psychological needs (Hermann et al., Table 3 [p.7]):

| Strategy | Competence threat | Autonomy threat | Relatedness threat |
|---|---|---|---|
| Direct resolution | Skill enhancement, workflow adaptation | Workflow adjustment to preserve final-decision authority | Collaboration with colleagues to learn GenAI together |
| Symbolic self-completion | Emphasis on irreplaceable human expertise | Emphasis on ownership and leadership in GenAI tasks | Emphasis on human-driven team contributions |
| Dissociation | Avoid GenAI-dependent tasks | Avoid GenAI-automated tasks | Prefer human-human teamwork |
| Escapism | Mental withdrawal, delegation to others | Extended breaks, reassignment requests | Isolation from GenAI-prominent settings |
| Fluid compensation | Shift to uniquely human skill domains | Shift to roles where decisions remain human-driven | Shift to customer-facing or human-centred roles |

## What To Do

[Inference] The following diagnostic procedure operationalizes Hermann et al.'s descriptive taxonomy into a step-by-step organizational practice. The five strategies and the strategy-by-need mapping are from the source; the procedure for using them as a diagnostic tool is a downstream extension, not directly prescribed in the paper.

For an organization or manager observing GenAI-related strain in workers:

1. **Identify which need is being frustrated** — competence, autonomy, or relatedness. Diagnostic question: what does the worker say they have lost? Capability and effectiveness (competence), control and self-direction (autonomy), or connection and belonging (relatedness)?

2. **Identify which strategy the worker is using** — direct resolution, symbolic self-completion, dissociation, escapism, or fluid compensation. Indicators: enrolment in training (1), prominence of credentials in communications (2), avoidance behaviours and reduced engagement in GenAI-mediated work (3), withdrawal patterns and rising absenteeism (4), shift in stated identity to alternative domain (5).

3. **Assess whether the strategy is productive in this context.** Direct resolution and (where the alternative domain is genuinely valuable) fluid compensation are typically productive. Symbolic self-completion is cheap and not actively harmful but does not resolve the underlying frustration. Dissociation and escapism reduce engagement and signal a need for organizational intervention.

4. **For maladaptive strategies, design interventions that address the source of the threat rather than the strategy itself.** Punishing escapism does not restore autonomy; providing autonomy-preserving deployment of GenAI does. Mocking symbolic self-completion does not restore competence; providing accessible upskilling pathways does.

5. **For workers using productive strategies, supply organizational support.** Direct resolution requires training time and access; fluid compensation requires the alternative domain to actually exist as a recognized, valued role.

## Why It Works

The five strategies are not idiosyncratic — they are documented across self-discrepancy research (Stinson et al., cited by Hermann et al.). What is novel here is the application to GenAI-induced workplace threats and the explicit mapping to the three basic psychological needs. Self-determination theory has strong empirical support (Ryan & Deci, 2017; Van den Broeck et al., 2016 meta-analysis), so the underlying need-frustration framing is well-grounded.

The taxonomy is **diagnostic**, not predictive: it provides a vocabulary for naming what workers are doing, but Hermann et al. explicitly flag (in their Outstanding Questions [p.9]) that we do not yet know which factors predict which strategy a worker will adopt in response to which threat. So the method is currently best used for naming and planning, not for predicting individual behaviour.

## Strengths / Limitations

**Strengths:**
- Names patterns the KB has been describing piecemeal (reactance, withdrawal, upskilling, identity shift) into a coherent five-strategy vocabulary.
- Maps cleanly onto the three basic needs, allowing diagnosis to flow from need-frustration to strategy.
- Distinguishes adaptive from maladaptive strategies with explicit cost analysis.
- Provides a frame for organizational interventions that channel workers toward productive strategies.

**Limitations:**
- The taxonomy is imported from self-discrepancy theory and applied analogically — Hermann et al. provide no GenAI-specific empirical demonstrations of any of the five strategies operating in workplace contexts. Treat as theoretical framework, not validated diagnostic tool.
- The five strategies are not necessarily mutually exclusive or exhaustive. A worker may simultaneously use direct resolution and symbolic self-completion; a sixth pattern (e.g., collective bargaining as in the Hollywood Writers' strike) may not fit the individual-level frame.
- Predicts which strategy is adaptive but does not predict which a given worker will use. Individual differences (perceived control, threat intensity, learning orientation, organizational support) likely moderate the choice; the taxonomy does not specify how.
- Maladaptive strategies (dissociation, escapism) may also be diagnoses of organizational failure rather than worker choice — the method risks pathologizing workers who are responding rationally to under-supportive environments.

## When It Applies

- Organizations introducing GenAI tools into established workflows where worker resistance, withdrawal, or identity-protection behaviours are visible.
- Managers diagnosing why a particular team or worker is engaging poorly with mandated GenAI integration.
- Workers themselves examining their own responses to GenAI-induced threats and considering whether their current strategy is productive in their context.

When NOT to use:
- As a substitute for engaging with the substantive concern. If a worker says GenAI is eroding their craft, classifying that as "symbolic self-completion" or "fluid compensation" without examining whether the concern is empirically valid is reductive.
- As a predictive tool. The framework explains what workers are doing; it does not reliably predict what a specific worker will do next.
