# AI Chatbot Effects on Loneliness: Longitudinal RCT

## Citation
Fang, C.M., Liu, A.R., Danry, V., Lee, E., Chan, S.W.T., Pataranutaporn, P., Maes, P., Phang, J., Lampe, M., Ahmad, L., & Agarwal, S. (2025). How AI and Human Behaviors Shape Psychosocial Effects of Chatbot Use: A Longitudinal Randomized Controlled Study. arXiv:2503.17473v1.

## Type
Paper

## Key Insight
In a 4-week RCT (N = 981, >300K messages), AI chatbot use showed complex dose-dependent psychosocial effects. Voice-based chatbots initially appeared beneficial (less loneliness, less emotional dependence vs. text), but these advantages diminished at high daily usage. Higher overall daily usage correlated with more loneliness, less socialization, greater emotional dependence, and more problematic AI use. Individual characteristics — attachment tendencies, trust in AI, prior chatbot use — strongly moderated outcomes.

## Relevance
Largest and longest controlled study of AI chatbot psychosocial effects to date. Extends the KB's focus beyond cognitive risks to include relational and emotional dimensions of AI interaction. Relevant for practitioners because the boundary between "AI as tool" and "AI as social presence" is increasingly blurred, especially with voice interfaces.

## Key Findings

**Usage and outcomes (all modalities, all conditions)**:
- Higher daily usage correlated with: more loneliness (p < 0.0001), less socialization with real people (p < 0.0001), higher emotional dependence on AI (p < 0.0001), more problematic AI use (p < 0.0001).
- Average usage: 5.32 minutes/day. Range: 1.01 to 27.65 minutes/day.

**Modality effects**:
- Voice chatbots initially showed benefits: less loneliness, less emotional dependence, less problematic use vs. text.
- At high daily usage, voice advantages disappeared. Neutral voice with prolonged use led to less socialization and more problematic use than text.
- Engaging voice chatbot was interacted with most (6.16 min/day vs. 4.35 min text).

**Conversation type effects**:
- Personal conversations slightly increased loneliness but decreased emotional dependence and problematic use.
- Non-personal conversations increased dependence among heavy users.
- Open-ended conversations had highest daily duration (6.02 min/day).

**Individual vulnerability factors**:
- Stronger attachment tendencies predicted more loneliness after 4 weeks of chatbot use.
- Higher trust in AI predicted greater emotional dependence.
- Prior companion chatbot use predicted higher dependence and problematic use.
- Women experienced more social withdrawal.
- Opposite-gender AI voice associated with more loneliness and emotional dependence.
- Emotional avoidance predicted more loneliness.

**Text modality paradox**: Text-based interactions showed highest emotional content (sharing problems, seeking support, alleviating loneliness) and highest self-disclosure. Yet text was also associated with most emotional dependence and problematic use at baseline comparison. More emotionally engaging interactions do not necessarily produce better outcomes.
