# MIT Media Lab (2025) — Your Brain on ChatGPT: Cognitive Debt

## Citation

Kosmyna, N., Hauptmann, E., Yuan, Y. T., Situ, J., Liao, X.-H., Beresnitzky, A. V., Braunstein, I., & Maes, P. (2025). *Your Brain on ChatGPT: Accumulation of Cognitive Debt when Using an AI Assistant for Essay Writing Task*. MIT Media Lab preprint. (CC BY-NC-SA)

**URL:** [media.mit.edu/publications/your-brain-on-chatgpt](https://www.media.mit.edu/publications/your-brain-on-chatgpt/)

## Type

Paper (EEG + NLP + interview study; 54 participants, 4 sessions over 4 months)

## Key Insight

The MIT Media Lab team ran an unusually well-instrumented study of AI-assisted writing: 54 participants split into three groups (LLM-only, Search-engine, Brain-only) wrote essays across **3 sessions**, with EEG recording brain activity throughout. In Session 4, group assignments swapped: LLM users were forced to write without AI (LLM-to-Brain), and Brain-only users were given AI (Brain-to-LLM). The four-method design (EEG + NLP analysis + interviews + scoring by both human teachers and an AI judge) lets the team triangulate effects across neural, linguistic, behavioral, and outcome dimensions.

The headline finding: **brain connectivity scales inversely with external support**. Brain-only participants showed the strongest, widest-ranging neural networks. Search-engine users showed intermediate engagement. LLM users showed the weakest overall coupling. The same pattern held across alpha, beta, theta, and delta frequency bands.

What the cross-over (Session 4) reveals is the *durability* of the effect:

- **LLM-to-Brain** (LLM users now writing alone) showed **weaker** neural connectivity than the always-Brain group — they didn't recover engagement immediately when the AI was removed. They also showed bias toward LLM-specific vocabulary in their unaided essays.
- **Brain-to-LLM** (Brain-only users now using AI) showed **higher** memory recall than LLM users had in earlier sessions, and re-engagement of broad neural regions during AI-assisted writing — they brought their already-developed engagement habits to the new tool.

The behavioral evidence is striking: **LLM users could not quote from their own essays just minutes after writing them**. They reported low ownership of their work. The "essay" they produced was something they hadn't really thought through.

The mechanism the paper names is **cognitive debt** — a loop where AI use → reduced engagement → weaker skill development → increased AI reliance → further weakness. Each session's debt compounds.

For human thinking with AI: this is the strongest neural evidence the KB has that AI substitution doesn't just affect performance, it affects the substrate of cognition itself. The cross-over data adds a stability dimension — habits of engagement, once weakened, don't snap back when the tool is removed.

## Relevance

The KB's strongest *neural* evidence for cognitive-offloading costs. Three contributions:

- **Multi-modal triangulation.** EEG + NLP + interview + scoring converges on the same conclusion across orthogonal measurement methods. Hard to dismiss as artifact of any single instrument.
- **Cross-over design isolates causation.** The Session 4 swap is the key methodological move. It shows that what looks like "AI users have weaker engagement" isn't a selection effect — it's an effect *of using the AI*. Removing the AI doesn't restore engagement immediately; the debt is durable.
- **Concrete failure-of-ownership signal.** "Can't quote your own essay minutes later" is a remarkably accessible diagnostic. Most users have an intuitive recognition of this experience.

## Open Questions

- N=54 (18 in Session 4) is small; replication needed.
- The study is essay-writing specific. Generalization to other knowledge work (coding, analysis, synthesis) is plausible but not yet tested.
- The cross-over effect (LLM-to-Brain participants don't recover engagement) raises the question of *recovery time*. Days? Weeks? Months? Permanent? The 4-month window doesn't answer.
- Individual differences: who's more vs. less susceptible to cognitive debt? The paper doesn't break results down by participant trait.
