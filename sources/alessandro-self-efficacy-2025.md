# AI Effects on Self-Efficacy, Self-Objectification, and Free Will Beliefs

## Citation

Alessandro, G., Federica, D., Baldissarri, C., Manfredi, A., Sterlicchio, A., & Romano, S. (2025). Artificial intelligence in the workplace: Effects on self-efficacy, self-objectification and beliefs in free will. *Journal of Community & Applied Social Psychology*, 35, e70107. https://doi.org/10.1002/casp.70107

## Type

Paper

## Key Insight

Being evaluated by an AI system (vs. a human) significantly lowers self-efficacy beliefs and increases self-objectification, which in turn reduces beliefs in free will. The effect occurs even in brief, scenario-based interactions and replicates with a real AI recruiting system. This establishes a causal pathway: AI evaluation --> reduced self-efficacy --> self-objectification --> diminished free will.

## Relevance

Provides the first empirical evidence that AI interactions can trigger self-objectification in work contexts. This is distinct from existing KB entries on identity threat (which focus on internal feeling of being "editor not creator") — here, being processed *by* AI makes people feel like objects. Connects workplace objectification literature to AI, opening a new erosion pathway relevant to anyone subject to AI-driven evaluation (hiring, performance review, monitoring).

## Key Findings

- **Consistent across 3 experiments** (~571 participants): AI recruiter condition vs. human recruiter
- **Self-efficacy**: Significantly lower when evaluated by AI vs. human
- **Self-objectification**: Significantly higher when evaluated by AI — participants attributed fewer mental states to themselves
- **Free will beliefs**: Reduced through the self-objectification pathway
- **Mediation chain**: AI interaction --> self-efficacy reduction --> self-objectification --> reduced free will beliefs
- **Ecological validity**: Study 2 used a real AI-based recruiting system (not just scenarios)
- **Theoretical grounding**: Builds on Baldissarri et al.'s work objectification framework and Bandura's self-efficacy theory
