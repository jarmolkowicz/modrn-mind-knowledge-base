# Folk, Heine & Dunn (2025) — Anthropomorphism and Social Connection to AI

## Citation

Folk, D., Heine, S. J., & Dunn, E. (2025). Individual differences in anthropomorphism help explain social connection to AI companions. *Scientific Reports*, 15, 36548.

**DOI:** [10.1038/s41598-025-19212-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-025-19212-2)

(University of British Columbia. Two-study experimental paper; Study 1 exploratory N = 240; Study 2 preregistered N = 1,034. Open data and R code on OSF: https://tinyurl.com/3y9kme29. Funded by SSHRC grant #F23-04301. Received 4 June 2025; accepted 7 September 2025.)

## Type

Paper (two between-subjects randomized experiments; Study 2 preregistered with addendum; brief format)

## Key Insight

Across two studies (total N = 1,274 American and Canadian Prolific participants), individual differences in *anthropomorphism of technology* — measured by an 8-item validated scale — moderate the immediate social-connection benefit of texting with a chatbot. Higher anthropomorphizers reported more state social connection after a 6-minute conversation with a ChatGPT-3.5-backed chatbot prompted to be warm and conversational; lower anthropomorphizers did not. Spotlight analyses identified anthropomorphism thresholds (3.13/6 in Study 1; 2.87/6 in Study 2) above which the chatbot beat journaling on connection — 58% (Study 1) and 72% (Study 2) of the sample exceeded that threshold. The pattern bridges a scholarly debate: AI's artificial nature *is* an obstacle to felt connection for some, but a minor one for those high in anthropomorphism.

## Key Passages

> "For some individuals, AI's artificial nature may pose an insurmountable barrier to meaningful connection; for others, however, this artificiality may be a minor obstacle, easily overcome by a tendency to anthropomorphize."
> — Folk, Heine & Dunn, [p.1, abstract]

> "People who scored higher in anthropomorphism of technology felt more connected after interacting with the chatbot (r = .33, p < .001) whereas anthropomorphism and connection were unrelated in the journal condition (r = .02, p = .792)."
> — Folk, Heine & Dunn, [p.2, Study 1 results]

> "Participants who scored above 2.87 (out of 6) on the anthropomorphism scale reported significantly higher levels of social connection in the chatbot condition compared to the journal condition (p < .05). This represents a large portion of our sample, as 72% of the participants scored above 2.87."
> — Folk, Heine & Dunn, [p.4, Study 2 spotlight]

> "If seeing human-like qualities in AI does foster connection, it likely is not through these two aspects of how people engage with chatbots. A remaining possibility, however, is that anthropomorphism fosters connection because it changes how people interpret the chatbot's responses, rather than how they themselves interact."
> — Folk, Heine & Dunn, [p.5, general discussion]

> "Anthropomorphism may buffer against the potential long-term costs because it makes interacting with AI more rewarding. However, people high in anthropomorphism could also be more likely to substitute AI interactions for human ones, potentially making them more susceptible to the long-term risks of AI companionship."
> — Folk, Heine & Dunn, [p.6, general discussion]

## Key Findings

**Sample and design**
- Two between-subjects experiments. Study 1 (Dec 2023): N = 240 American Prolific workers; mean age 38.94, 55% female. Study 2 (May 2024): N = 1,034 American + Canadian Prolific workers (20% Canadian); mean age 36.61, 53% female. Total N = 1,274.
- Random assignment to chatbot condition (texting a ChatGPT-3.5-API chatbot prompted to "respond in a very happy and positive manner and try to keep the conversation flowing by asking follow-up questions" about the past month) or journal condition (writing about the past month). Both ran for at least 6 minutes.
- 8-item Anthropomorphism of Technology measure (Folk, Wu & Heine, 2025; α = 0.88; example item: "AI's have the capacity to become conscious beings"; 1–6 scale). Distinct from but correlated (r ≈ .40) with general anthropomorphism (Epley, Waytz & Cacioppo, 2007 / Waytz, Cacioppo & Epley, 2010).
- Single-item state social-connection outcome ("Compared to how socially connected you normally feel, how socially connected do you feel right now?" −5 to +5). Single item chosen because multi-item measures of state social connection (Lok & Dunn, 2023) reference humans, which would be interpreted differently across conditions.
- Study 2 preregistered (sample size raised from N = 580 to N = 1,000 prior to data collection); Study 1 exploratory.

**Headline regression (Table 1, [p.3])**
- Study 1: Condition main effect B = 0.154, p = .014; Anthropomorphism (centered) B = 0.024, p = .785 (n.s.); Condition × Anthropomorphism interaction B = 0.218, p = .013; R² = 0.081.
- Study 2: Condition main effect B = 0.099, p < .001; Anthropomorphism main effect B = 0.299, p < .001; Condition × Anthropomorphism interaction B = 0.100, p = .013; R² = 0.144.
- Interaction significant in both studies; both surviving controls for age, gender, and (Study 2) prior chatbot use (p = .021).

**Mean-level comparison**
- Study 1: chatbot M = 1.48 (SD = 2.12) vs. journal M = 0.79 (SD = 2.15); Cohen's d = 0.32.
- Study 2: chatbot M = 1.46 (SD = 2.20) vs. journal M = 1.14 (SD = 2.08); t(1027.8) = 2.39, p = .008 (one-tailed); Cohen's d = 0.15. Smaller effect than Study 1; authors attribute the shrinkage primarily to higher journal-condition connection in Study 2 ([p.3]).

**Within-condition correlations**
- Study 1 chatbot: anthropomorphism × connection r = .33, p < .001. Study 1 journal: r = .02, p = .792.
- Study 2 chatbot: r = .427, p < .001. Study 2 journal: r = .310, p < .001 — unlike Study 1, anthropomorphism *also* correlated with connection during journaling, though more weakly.

**Spotlight (Johnson-Neyman) analyses**
- Study 1: anthropomorphism > 3.13/6 → chatbot beat journal on connection; 58% of sample exceeded threshold.
- Study 2: anthropomorphism > 2.87/6 → chatbot beat journal; 72% of sample exceeded threshold.

**Exploratory text analysis (Study 2)**
- ChatGPT-4o-mini rated participants' messages/journal entries on warmth/positivity and self-disclosure (Rathje et al. 2024 pipeline).
- Journaling produced higher warmth (M = 3.32 vs. 3.04, d = 0.31) and higher self-disclosure (M = 2.91 vs. 2.44, d = 0.60) than chatbot interaction. Both ps < .001. ([p.4–5], Table 2).
- Across conditions, warmth correlated with social connection (r = .26, p < .001) but disclosure did not (r = .02, p = .500). No significant Condition × warmth or Condition × disclosure interactions.
- Anthropomorphism × warmth r = .17, p < .001; anthropomorphism × disclosure r = .12, p < .001 — relationships did not differ by condition.
- Net: differences in warmth/disclosure across conditions cannot explain why chatbot users felt more connected; warmth/disclosure also do not mediate the anthropomorphism × condition interaction.

**Authors' interpretation of the interaction mechanism**
- Two engagement candidates (warmth, self-disclosure) failed to mediate the effect.
- Authors propose an *interpretive* mechanism: anthropomorphizers may interpret the chatbot's responses as more genuinely understanding and validating, deriving more connection from the same input. Untested in this paper.
- Study 2's anthropomorphism-journal correlation (absent in Study 1) is interpreted via mental imagery: high anthropomorphizers may have richer imagined moments with friends and family while journaling (Tahiroglu & Taylor 2019; Severson & Woodard 2018), boosting felt connection.

## Relevance

Three load-bearing contributions:

- **Names and empirically anchors the [[anthropomorphism-of-technology]] moderator** — the individual-difference variable the KB's loneliness/companion cluster has been pointing at without housing. Folk-Heine-Dunn supply the construct (an 8-item scale, α = 0.88, validated in Folk, Wu & Heine 2025), the experimental evidence that it moderates immediate social-connection benefit from chatbots, and the spotlight analyses that quantify how much of the population sits above the threshold (58–72%).
- **Locates the entry-point on the [[ai-loneliness-effect]] dose-response curve.** Where Fang's 4-week RCT shows population-average dose-dependent effects and where the sibling [[folk-dunn-companionship-loneliness-2026]] tracks bidirectional dynamics over 12 months, Folk-Heine-Dunn answer "for whom does the entry-point reward fire?" — a question the immediate-benefit literature (Folk, Yu & Dunn 2024; De Freitas et al. 2024; Drouin et al. 2022) had not isolated. The result links *who feels rewarded immediately* to *who is at risk of escalating* in the longitudinal design.
- **Sharpens [[mira-model]] principle 2 (psychological proximity) into a moderator-driven claim.** MIRA names psychological proximity — the felt sense of closeness produced by AI's adaptive responsiveness — as a relational mechanism, but Boyd & Markowitz frame it as a property of the interaction. Folk-Heine-Dunn supply evidence that the *user's* anthropomorphism trait determines whether that proximity translates to felt connection. The interaction is not symmetric: identical AI responses generate different connection depending on the user's tendency to perceive a mind in them.

## Contradicts / Extends

- **Extends** [[folk-dunn-companionship-loneliness-2026]] (the sibling longitudinal paper) with a population entry-point answer to the question that paper raises in its general discussion — *who* is most likely to be drawn into the bidirectional substitution dynamic. The two papers triangulate: this paper isolates entry-point individual differences in immediate reward; the sibling tracks dynamics over months. Same first author and senior author (Dunn); different middle author (Heine vs. solo-Dunn).
- **Extends** [[fang-ai-loneliness-2025]]'s individual-vulnerability findings (attachment tendencies, AI trust) with a different and arguably upstream moderator — anthropomorphism — that may explain why some users show stronger initial reward and therefore stronger dose-response trajectories.
- **Refines** the customer-service anthropomorphism literature (Blut et al. 2021; Sheehan et al. 2020) which had focused on AI design (more vs. less anthropomorphized AI) using hypothetical-interaction studies. Folk-Heine-Dunn show that *user-side* individual differences explain divergent reactions to the *same* AI — a complementary level of analysis.
- Consistent with the De Freitas et al. (2024) finding that texting AI reduces loneliness on average; Folk-Heine-Dunn refine the average effect by showing how it concentrates in the upper half of the anthropomorphism distribution.

## Open Questions

- **Mechanism of the anthropomorphism × chatbot interaction.** The authors rule out warmth and self-disclosure as mediators and propose an *interpretive* mechanism (anthropomorphizers feel more genuinely understood by the same chatbot output). Untested. Future work should test whether feeling-understood mediates.
- **Long-term consequences for high anthropomorphizers.** The authors flag the open question explicitly: does anthropomorphism *buffer* against the long-term substitution costs documented by [[folk-dunn-companionship-loneliness-2026]] (because the rewards keep coming) or *amplify* them (because high anthropomorphizers are more likely to substitute AI for human contact)? The answer matters for risk profiling.
- **Generalizability beyond Anglophone Western Prolific samples.** Both studies sampled from US/Canadian Prolific. Folk, Wu & Heine (2025) found cultural variation in attitudes toward social chatbots; whether the moderator effect is similar magnitude in non-Western samples is open.
- **Anthropomorphism vs. mental-imagery vividness as the latent variable.** The Study 2 anthropomorphism-journal correlation hints that anthropomorphism's effect on social connection may operate partly via richer mental imagery rather than through AI-specific perception. Untangling these would clarify the construct.
- **Causal direction of the anthropomorphism-connection relationship.** Single-timepoint experimental design with a trait moderator: could connection-readiness predict anthropomorphism rather than (or in addition to) the reverse? Folk et al. (2025) found no anthropomorphism × trait-loneliness correlation, partially addressing one direction but not exhausting the question.
- **Voice and multimodal AI.** Both studies used text-based chatbots. Voice and embodied chatbots may interact with anthropomorphism differently — both because the channel itself signals more "humanness" and because high anthropomorphizers may extract more from human-like voice cues than from text.
