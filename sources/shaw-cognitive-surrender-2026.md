# Thinking Fast, Slow, and Artificial (Shaw & Nave, 2026)

## Citation

Shaw, S. D., & Nave, G. (2026). Thinking fast, slow, and artificial: How AI is reshaping human reasoning and the rise of cognitive surrender. The Wharton School of the University of Pennsylvania. SSRN Working Paper.

## Type

Paper (preprint)

## Key Insight

When people have access to AI, they frequently adopt its outputs with minimal scrutiny — a phenomenon the authors call cognitive surrender. Across three preregistered experiments (N=1,372), AI access boosted accuracy by +25pp when AI was correct but reduced it by -15pp when AI erred, with an overall effect size of Cohen's h = 0.81. Confidence increased even when AI was wrong. The pattern persisted under time pressure and was only partially attenuated by incentives and feedback.

## Relevance

Provides the first structured experimental evidence for how AI displaces internal reasoning processes, with a clear theoretical framework (Tri-System Theory) and measurable individual-difference predictors. Directly relevant to the KB's core concerns: cognitive sovereignty, erosion risk, and the distinction between adaptive and maladaptive AI use.

## Key Findings

- Participants consulted AI on >50% of trials when available
- Followed faulty AI advice on ~80% of chat-engaged trials (Study 1)
- AI access inflated confidence by ~12pp regardless of AI accuracy
- Time pressure did not eliminate surrender (Study 2)
- Incentives + item-level feedback doubled override rates on faulty trials but did not eliminate the surrender pattern (Study 3)
- Higher trust in AI, lower need for cognition, and lower fluid intelligence predicted greater surrender
- On AI-faulty trials: 73.2% showed surrender, 19.7% showed offloading, 7.1% were failed overrides
