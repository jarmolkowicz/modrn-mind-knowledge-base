# Schilke & Reimann (2025) — The Transparency Dilemma

## Citation

Schilke, O., & Reimann, M. (2025). The transparency dilemma: How AI disclosure erodes trust. *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, 188, 104405.

**DOI:** [10.1016/j.obhdp.2025.104405](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.obhdp.2025.104405)

**Note on authorship:** Schilke is corresponding author; the slug in this KB (`reimann-schilke-...`) reflects an earlier press-release framing but the published paper lists Schilke first. Slug retained for wikilink continuity; citation shows the correct order.

## Type

Paper (13-experiment empirical bundle, 5,000+ participants)

## Key Insight

Across **13 pre-registered experiments** spanning tasks (writing, analytics, art, hiring, financial services), actor types (supervisors, subordinates, professors, analysts, creatives, investment funds), and disclosure conditions, **actors who disclose their AI usage are trusted less than those who do not**. The trust penalty:

- holds **across diverse tasks** and actor roles,
- holds **above and beyond algorithm aversion** (it isn't just discomfort with AI itself),
- holds whether disclosure is **voluntary or mandatory**,
- holds **across different disclosure framings**, including soft language ("only used for grammar checking", "human-reviewed"),
- is **larger when undisclosed AI use is exposed by a third party** than when disclosed voluntarily — i.e., getting caught is worse than transparency, but transparency still costs trust.

The proposed mechanism is **legitimacy**, drawing on micro-institutional theory: AI disclosure signals deviation from the normative expectation that work outputs reflect "human expertise, judgment, and reasoning." That perceived legitimacy violation is what drives the trust loss, not algorithm aversion or productivity skepticism. Studies 6–8 establish this mechanism via mediation analysis.

A within-paper meta-analysis shows the trust penalty is **attenuated but not eliminated** by favorable technology attitudes and high perceived AI accuracy — and crucially, was **not attenuated** among evaluators who themselves use AI or are AI-familiar. The penalty doesn't dissolve as AI diffuses.

For human thinking with AI: this is the strongest empirical evidence the KB has for [[disclosure-penalty]] and [[transparency-paradox]]. It shifts the framing of "should I disclose?" from a binary ethics question to a **trade-off between two costs**: voluntary disclosure costs some trust; getting caught costs more trust. There is no zero-cost option; disclosure is just the *less* costly path *if exposure is plausible*.

## Relevance

The most rigorous empirical study to date on the AI disclosure–trust relationship. Three contributions for the KB:

- **Robustness.** 13 experiments × 5,000+ participants × multiple tasks × multiple actor types — this is among the most replicated effects in AI-and-trust literature. The KB's [[disclosure-penalty]] entry should treat this as the foundational source.
- **Mechanism.** The legitimacy-as-mediator finding moves the conversation past "people are uncomfortable with AI" toward a structural account: AI disclosure violates a norm about *human-only outputs*, and norm-violation is what triggers trust erosion. This generalizes — the same dynamic should apply wherever a similar normative expectation exists (artistry, scholarship, journalism).
- **Persistence.** The penalty doesn't shrink as familiarity grows. This contradicts the "AI use will become normalized" prediction and supports the KB's framing that disclosure is a structural problem, not a transition cost.

Complementary to [[cheong-penalizing-transparency-2025]], which adds the demographic-interaction dimension (LLM raters' "vanishing alignment") that Schilke & Reimann's design didn't include.

## Contradicts / Extends

- Extends [[cheong-penalizing-transparency-2025]] — both find a disclosure penalty; Schilke & Reimann establish the legitimacy mechanism, while Cheong et al. establish the demographic-interaction asymmetry under LLM raters.
- Conflicts with the prevailing transparency-is-always-good narrative in tech ethics. The paper is explicit that "the effect of transparency may be more contingent than commonly assumed" and demonstrates a domain where transparency backfires.
- Parallels conflict-of-interest disclosure literature (Sah & Feiler 2020; Loewenstein et al. 2011) — disclosure of personal/financial COIs has been found to reduce trust by similar mechanisms; AI disclosure is a new instance of an older pattern.

## Open Questions

- The legitimacy mechanism predicts that as norms shift toward expecting AI involvement, the penalty should fade. Schilke & Reimann's data so far show no fade — but their data is from 2024–2025. Will follow-up studies in 2027–2028 show movement?
- Does the penalty differ by *what* the AI did (drafted vs. edited vs. fact-checked vs. translated)? The disclosure manipulations in this paper are mostly binary (used / didn't use); a more granular dose-response would clarify which AI involvements specifically violate legitimacy.
- For collaborative work with explicit AI co-authorship norms (e.g., software engineering), is there a sub-domain where the penalty inverts?
