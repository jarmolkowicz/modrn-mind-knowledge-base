# Knowing Enough to Be Dangerous: Artificial Certainty

## Citation

Leonardi, P. M., & Leavell, V. (2026). Knowing Enough to Be Dangerous: The Problem of "Artificial Certainty" for Expert Authority When Using AI for Decision Making and Planning. *Organization Science*, Articles in Advance, 1-28. https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.2023.18224

## Type

Paper (Comparative Ethnography)

## Key Insight

AI-generated representations create "artificial certainty" — the illusion that inherently uncertain futures are definitively knowable. Through comparative study of two urban planning organizations using the same AI simulation tool, the paper identifies three representational practices (controlling detail, shaping engagement, constructing meaning) and two deployment modes: enhancement (amplifying AI capabilities, which undermines expert authority) and modulation (tempering AI outputs, which preserves expert authority and useful uncertainty).

## Relevance

Extends the KB's individual-level concepts ([[confidence-competence-gap]], [[borrowed-certainty]]) to the organizational level. Shows that AI doesn't just affect individual cognition — it restructures institutional authority and decision-making by making non-experts feel they "know enough to be dangerous." Published in *Organization Science*, providing organizational theory grounding for phenomena the KB has documented at the individual level.

## Key Findings

- Three representational practices shape stakeholder perception of certainty: controlling detail level, shaping stakeholder engagement, constructing the model's meaning
- Enhancement mode: experts amplify AI capabilities, stakeholders mistake representations for reality, expert authority is undermined
- Modulation mode: experts temper AI outputs, preserve appropriate uncertainty, maintain authority as mediators
- Critical distinction: "representations *of* the future" (claiming predictive certainty) vs. "representations *for* the future" (supporting deliberation)
- Process expertise is reframed as interpretive work that maintains useful levels of uncertainty
