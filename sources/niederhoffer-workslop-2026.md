# Workslop (Niederhoffer et al.)

## Citation
Niederhoffer, K., Robichaux, A., & Hancock, J. T. (2025). AI-Generated Workslop Is Destroying Productivity. *Harvard Business Review*.

Niederhoffer, K., Robichaux, A., & Hancock, J. T. (2026). Why People Create AI "Workslop" — and How to Stop It. *Harvard Business Review*.

## Type
Article (two-part series, BetterUp Labs / Stanford Social Media Lab)

## Key Insight
Workslop is AI-generated work content that "masquerades as good work, but lacks the substance to meaningfully advance a given task." Unlike traditional cognitive offloading (which outsources mental work to a machine), workslop uses machines to offload cognitive work to another human being — the recipient must decode, infer context, and often redo the work. It is not a technology failure but a management failure driven by vague AI mandates and overwhelmed teams.

## Relevance
Provides the first large-scale survey data (N=1,150) on the organizational cost of undiscerning AI use. Bridges individual cognitive risks (fluency bias, novice vulnerability) to organizational dynamics (trust erosion, collaboration breakdown). Directly relevant to workslop as a downstream consequence of the confidence-competence gap.

## Key Findings (N=1,150 U.S. full-time employees)

### Prevalence
- 40% report receiving workslop in the last month
- 53% admit to sending workslop at least some of the time
- 1 in 10 say 50%+ of AI work they send is unhelpful/low-quality
- Flows between peers (40%), upward from reports (18%), downward from managers (16%)
- Professional services and technology disproportionately affected

### Costs
- Average 1 hour 56 minutes per incident dealing with workslop
- Estimated $186/month invisible tax per affected employee
- $9M+/year for a 10,000-person organization

### Social/Emotional Impact
- 53% annoyed, 38% confused, 22% offended
- ~50% view workslop senders as less creative, capable, and reliable
- 42% see sender as less trustworthy; 37% as less intelligent
- 34% notify teammates or managers
- 32% less likely to want to work with sender again

### Root Causes (Article 2)
- 41% of employees report leadership encourages AI use without detailed instructions
- Workslop emerges from pressure at both ends: boards pushing ROI, employees overburdened
- 53% of respondents admit sending subpar AI work — primarily because they feel "stretched too thin"
- Blanket AI mandates without training, agency, or cultural trust encourage performative AI use
- "Workslop is both a symptom and potential accelerant" of deeper organizational issues

### Protective Factors
- Competence and control over AI tools: half as likely to create workslop
- Team trust: reduced workslop by 61%
- "Pilots" (high agency + high optimism) use AI 75% more often but use it to enhance creativity; "Passengers" use AI to avoid work

## Key Quote
"The greatest irony of all is that to make AI work at work, we need to get better at being human."
