# The Moralization of Artificial Intelligence

## Citation

de Mello, V. O., Côté, É., Ayad, R., Inbar, Y., Plaks, J., & Inzlicht, M. (2026). The moralization of artificial intelligence. University of Toronto. Code and datasets: https://osf.io/64kbf/

## Type

Paper

## Key Insight

AI resistance is partly moral in nature — people oppose AI not merely because they find it impractical, but because they see it as fundamentally wrong. This moral opposition generalizes across AI applications, is consequence-insensitive, and predicts real behavioral reluctance to use AI even when personally beneficial (1 SD increase in moralization = 42% decrease in AI usage).

## Relevance

Opens a new dimension in the KB. Existing entries treat AI resistance through pragmatic lenses (disclosure penalty, transparency paradox, professional identity threat). This paper shows that for a meaningful segment of the public, resistance is moral — rooted in convictions about right and wrong, not cost-benefit analysis. This has direct implications for how educators and practitioners frame AI adoption conversations.

## Key Findings

- **Media analysis** (69,890 headlines, 2018-2024): AI moralized at levels comparable to GMOs and vaccines — both technologies known for moral polarization
- **Moralization spike**: Sharp increase in AI moralization between 2021-2023, coinciding with DALL-E and ChatGPT launches
- **Consequence insensitivity**: Most AI opponents say their views would not change even if AI proved beneficial
- **Single latent factor**: AI moralization is generalized — not specific to particular applications (art, law, medicine)
- **Behavioral prediction**: Earlier moralization scores predicted AI avoidance 75-573 days later, even at personal cost
- **Pragmatic masking**: Moralizers typically justify opposition with pragmatic language, making moral resistance harder to detect
