# Runco & Jaeger (2012) — The Standard Definition of Creativity

## Citation

Runco, M. A., & Jaeger, G. J. (2012). The standard definition of creativity. *Creativity Research Journal*, 24(1), 92–96.

**DOI:** [10.1080/10400419.2012.650092](https://doi.org/10.1080/10400419.2012.650092)

## Type

Paper (definitional / historical correction)

## Key Insight

Runco & Jaeger formalize what creativity researchers had been using informally for decades: **creativity requires both originality and effectiveness** (also labeled usefulness, fit, appropriateness, or value). Either alone is not creativity.

- *Originality without effectiveness* = "the word salad of a psychotic" or random output from monkeys on word processors. Novel, but not creative.
- *Effectiveness without originality* = conventional, mundane, commonplace.

Their historiographic argument: the bipartite definition appears first as a clear formulation in Stein (1953), with Barron (1955) close behind, despite earlier gestures toward the same idea by Bethune (1839), Royce (1898), Hutchinson (1931), and Guilford (1950).

The paper also flags two unresolved questions: (a) **who judges effectiveness** ("Who is to judge the judges?", Murray 1958) — internal vs. external frames of reference matter; (b) **whether two criteria are enough** — Simonton (2012) argues for a third (surprise, derived from U.S. Patent Office criteria); Runco himself elsewhere wondered whether one suffices.

For human thinking with AI: this is the standard against which "AI creativity" claims should be measured. LLMs generate novel-looking output abundantly (originality is solved), but *effectiveness* — fitness for a specific context, audience, problem — requires understanding what counts as appropriate, which is the harder half. The bipartite frame predicts that AI-generated novelty without contextual judgment lands in Runco & Jaeger's "merely original" category — interesting-looking but not actually creative. The KB's [[creativity-diversity-paradox]] concept is the at-scale version of the same point: AI can boost individual originality while undermining collective effectiveness through homogenization.

## Key Passages

> "The standard definition is bipartite: Creativity requires both originality and effectiveness."
> — Runco & Jaeger, [p.2]

> "Originality is vital for creativity but is not sufficient. Ideas and products that are merely original might very well be useless. They may be unique or uncommon for good reason! Originality can be found in the word salad of a psychotic and can be produced by monkeys on word processors. A truly random process will often generate something that is merely original."
> — Runco & Jaeger, [p.2]

> "Effectiveness may take the form of value… [Bethune 1839] described value as: 'The stability of the fabric which gives perpetuity to the decoration. To mingle the useful with the beautiful, is the highest style of art.'"
> — Runco & Jaeger, [p.2-3]

> "The standard definition only pinpoints which criteria must be used; it does not say anything about who is to judge each, and who is to judge the judges."
> — Runco & Jaeger, [p.5]

> "[Stein 1953]: 'The creative work is a novel work that is accepted as tenable or useful or satisfying by a group in some point in time.'"
> — Runco & Jaeger, [p.4] (quoting Stein 1953)

## Relevance

The KB's anchor for *what creativity actually is* — load-bearing for any claim the KB makes about AI's effects on creative work. Three contributions:

- **Disqualifies the "AI creativity" shortcut.** A conversation about whether AI is creative collapses to whether the output is *effective*, not whether it's novel. The novelty side is uncontested; the effectiveness side is where the actual question lives.
- **Locates the human role.** If effectiveness requires "judges" with a specific frame of reference, then the human's role in AI-assisted creative work is precisely the judging. The author who picks which AI-generated draft to ship, why, and for whom *is* doing the creative work — the AI is doing the originality work.
- **Frames the AI-and-creativity research literature.** The KB's [[creativity-diversity-paradox]] (Doshi & Hauser 2024 etc.) shows AI raises individual quality (where quality bundles originality+effectiveness) but homogenizes collective output. Runco & Jaeger's framework explains *why* that's possible: AI can supply originality but inherits effectiveness criteria from training data, so collective effectiveness narrows.

## Contradicts / Extends

- Extended by Simonton's three-criterion proposal (originality + effectiveness + surprise, derived from U.S. Patent Office) and by Runco's later one-criterion parsimony argument. The bipartite definition is the working consensus; alternative criterion counts remain in active debate.
- Provides the framework that [[creativity-diversity-paradox]] (Doshi & Hauser, *Science Advances* 2024) and Anderson et al. (2024) operationalize at scale.

## Open Questions

- *Who judges effectiveness?* Runco & Jaeger flag this as unresolved. In AI-assisted work, is the relevant judge the human author, the audience, the platform algorithm, or the AI itself? Each gives a different answer to "is this creative?"
- Does AI-generated output ever meet the "effectiveness" bar without human curation, or is human judgment of fit always the load-bearing step?
- The standard definition was developed pre-LLM. Does "novelty" mean the same thing when an LLM has already seen 99% of human creative output? "Novel to whom" matters more than the framework's authors anticipated.
