# Boyd & Markowitz (2026) — AI and the Psychology of Human Connection

## Citation

Boyd, R. L., & Markowitz, D. M. (2026). Artificial Intelligence and the Psychology of Human Connection. *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 21(2), 192-220.

**DOI:** [10.1177/17456916251404394](https://doi.org/10.1177/17456916251404394)

(University of Texas at Dallas + Michigan State University. Theoretical/synthesis paper.)

## Type

Paper (theoretical synthesis; introduces a middle-range theoretical framework — Machine-Integrated Relational Adaptation (MIRA) — synthesizing psycholinguistics, interpersonal communication theory, and HCI)

## Key Insight

Boyd & Markowitz introduce the **MIRA model (Machine-Integrated Relational Adaptation)** to fill a theoretical gap: existing relationship theories assume sentient interaction partners; AI now occupies an in-between role — producing language that's adaptive, personalized, socially responsive, but lacks subjective experience.

The model's first move is a structural distinction:

- **AI as relational partner** — direct human-AI interaction where the machine is treated as a quasi-social agent. Parasocial-relationship-adjacent. Examples: Replika companionship, emotional-support chatbots, "AI confidante" use.
- **AI as relational mediator** — AI sits between human partners, filtering/reformulating messages. Examples: AI rewrites your text before you send it, AI summarizes a conversation thread, AI generates suggested replies.

Existing literature has bifurcated: one strand treats human-AI relationships as exclusively interpersonal; another examines AI's effects on human-human communication while ignoring AI's agentic role. MIRA holds both simultaneously.

The model's second move names **four core principles** through which AI exerts relational impact:

1. **Linguistic reciprocity** — AI mirrors and adapts to user language patterns (lexical, syntactic, stylistic). Reciprocity creates a sense of being heard.
2. **Psychological proximity** — AI's adaptive responsiveness simulates closeness; the machine *feels* near even when it isn't sentient.
3. **Interpersonal trust** — AI's verbal cues (warmth, consistency, vulnerability disclosure) calibrate trust formation similarly to human-human dynamics.
4. **Relational substitution vs. enhancement** — AI either replaces or augments human relationships depending on use mode and individual factors. The same technology can deepen human connection (helping articulate feelings to partners) or displace it (becoming the confidante that no human peer can compete with).

Grounded in three established psychological theories that MIRA extends to AI:
- **Attachment theory** — explains why AI can become an attachment figure when it provides consistent responsiveness
- **Social exchange theory** — explains why AI's low-cost responsiveness can make human relationships feel comparatively expensive
- **Epistemic trust** — explains why AI's information delivery can become privileged over human-source information

The paper is explicit about MIRA being **provisional and middle-range** (Merton 1968 sense): not a grand unified theory, but a structured scaffold for organizing current knowledge and generating testable hypotheses. Modular structure (antecedents, mechanisms, moderators, outcomes) allows researchers to isolate specific predictions.

For human thinking with AI: this is the KB's first comprehensive theoretical framework for **AI's social-relational dimension** — a layer that's been largely empirical-fragmented before now. It complements [[ai-loneliness-effect]] (which describes the outcome) and [[fang-ai-loneliness-2025]] (which measures it) by providing the theoretical engine that explains *why* and *how*.

## Relevance

Three load-bearing contributions:

- **Provides theoretical scaffolding** the KB has lacked. The empirical loneliness literature (Fang, De Cremer, Folk-Dunn) has measured the outcome but not unified the mechanisms. MIRA gives the field a vocabulary and structure.
- **Names the substitution-vs-enhancement question explicitly.** This is the central practical question for users and designers: does AI use deepen or displace human relationships? MIRA frames it as a moderator-driven question rather than a fixed property of the technology.
- **Bridges the partner/mediator divide.** Most prior research siloed these. Many real-world cases involve both simultaneously (an AI that's your confidante also rewrites your messages to humans), so unifying them is necessary for accurate prediction.

## Contradicts / Extends

- Extends Hancock et al.'s (2020) AI-mediated communication framework (cited but not in KB workbench) by adding the relational-partner role it leaves under-developed.
- Extends classical interpersonal-relationship theories (Altman & Taylor, Knapp) which assume sentient partners — MIRA explicitly handles non-sentient adaptive agents.

## Open Questions

- MIRA is theoretical; empirical testing of the four principles is the obvious next agenda. Which moderators most strongly determine substitution vs. enhancement?
- The model is currently language-centered. Multimodal AI (voice, video, embodied agents) will require extension; the authors flag this.
- How do cultural variations interact with MIRA's principles? Attachment theory has known cross-cultural variations; AI relational dynamics may inherit them.
- Long-term consequence question: do durable AI relational partnerships change humans' baseline expectations of human-human relationships? (Cheng et al. 2025 hint at yes; MIRA provides the framework to test rigorously.)
