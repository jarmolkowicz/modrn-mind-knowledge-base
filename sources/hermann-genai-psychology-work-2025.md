# Hermann, Puntoni & Morewedge (2025) — GenAI and the Psychology of Work

## Citation

Hermann, E., Puntoni, S., & Morewedge, C. K. (2025). GenAI and the psychology of work. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2025.04.009

(Hermann at ESCP Business School Berlin; Puntoni at Wharton; Morewedge at Boston University Questrom. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* Opinion piece, ~9,000 words.)

## Type

Paper (theoretical synthesis / Opinion in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*; integrates ~100 references rather than reporting new data)

## Key Insight

Hermann, Puntoni & Morewedge organize the GenAI-and-work literature through **Self-Determination Theory (SDT)** and **Basic Psychological Needs Theory (BPNT)**: workers' wellbeing, motivation, and performance hinge on satisfying three basic needs — competence, autonomy, and relatedness — and GenAI can either support or frustrate each. Need-frustration produces psychological threats (self-discrepancies between actual and ideal capability, control, and connectedness), and workers respond with five compensatory strategies adapted from self-discrepancy research: **direct resolution, symbolic self-completion, dissociation, escapism, and fluid compensation**. The synthesis lens is the contribution; the underlying empirical claims it cites are second-hand.

## Key Passages

> "GenAI can benefit workers by enhancing their productivity and performance. It can also psychologically threaten workers' needs for competence, autonomy, and relatedness, which can initiate five coping strategies to mitigate these threats."
> — Hermann, Puntoni & Morewedge, [p.1] (abstract)

> "GenAI's creative, analytical, and communicative affordances can foster a sense of professional inadequacy and make workers feel less competent. Automating routine tasks and supporting more complex tasks can reduce opportunities for workers to use and advance their capabilities and skills, leading to perceptions of deskilling and stagnation in professional growth."
> — [p.4]

> "The more workers outsource tasks to GenAI without fully understanding or mastering those tasks, the more GenAI could undermine their foundational skills (e.g., critical problem-solving) and professional growth."
> — [pp.4-5]

> "High-skilled workers tend to overestimate their own expertise and might consider GenAI as unsuitable for enhancing their capabilities and work, creating a 'paradox of expertise'. Experts disregard and do not adopt and use GenAI due to their self-perceived deep domain knowledge and high skill level."
> — [p.5]

> "Autonomy needs can be further frustrated when GenAI integration is mandatory and not voluntary, and GenAI systems become an 'algorithmic cage'. GenAI-imposed workflows that standardize procedures and prioritize efficiency over flexibility and adaptability hamper the ability to tailor work to personal or situational needs."
> — [p.6]

> "Working with GenAI can erode social norms in ways that deteriorate interactions with other humans and impressions of human work. If GenAI integration is widespread and salient it can increase the objectification of other workers. If perceptions of workers are assimilated toward the GenAI systems on which they rely, coworkers can be dehumanized."
> — [pp.6-7]

> "Workers consider GenAI use to be more acceptable for themselves than for their colleagues. More generally, higher-skilled, more experienced workers perceive that less-skilled, younger workers use GenAI in meaningless and unintended ways, whereas lower-skilled, younger workers perceive that higher-skilled workers adhere to pre-GenAI practices and routines."
> — [p.7]

> "Five such compensatory strategies have been identified: direct resolution, symbolic self-completion, dissociation, escapism, and fluid compensation. We suggest these strategies provide a framework for understanding how workers respond to GenAI-induced threats resulting from the frustration of their basic psychological needs."
> — [p.7]

> "Symbolic self-completion may be the least costly strategy for workers, as it involves relatively low effort and resources to regain a sense of competence, autonomy, and relatedness. Direct resolution and fluid compensation, however, may require workers to expend more time and personal resources to address the sources of the threat. […] In contrast, dissociation and escapism can help workers immediately distance themselves from the source of threat, but these strategies may come at the cost of reduced engagement, missed opportunities for personal growth, and a diminished sense of connection to their work."
> — [p.8]

## Relevance

Three load-bearing contributions to the KB:

- **BPNT/SDT umbrella.** The KB has built entries on competence (cognitive-debt, novice-vulnerability, performance-paradox), autonomy (agency, situational-disempowerment), and relatedness (ai-loneliness-effect, social-friction) without naming the integrating frame. Hermann et al. supply it: these are not three independent risk classes but three faces of one psychological structure (basic needs at work). The frame is well-validated outside AI research (Ryan & Deci, 2017; Van den Broeck et al., 2016 meta-analysis). For practitioners, this lets a single diagnostic question — "which of competence, autonomy, or relatedness is being frustrated here?" — replace ad-hoc reasoning across a dozen risk-side concepts.

- **Five-strategy coping taxonomy.** The KB has been describing worker responses to AI ("editor not creator," reactance, withdrawal, upskilling) without a structured vocabulary. Hermann et al. import five compensatory strategies from self-discrepancy theory and map them to the three needs (Table 3). The taxonomy distinguishes adaptive responses (direct resolution, fluid compensation, sometimes symbolic self-completion) from maladaptive ones (dissociation, escapism), with explicit cost analysis — direct resolution requires resources the organization may need to provide; symbolic self-completion is cheap but does not address the source; dissociation and escapism come at engagement cost. This is operationally useful for diagnosing how workers are responding and for designing interventions that channel them toward productive strategies.

- **Paradox of expertise.** A labelled construct the KB does not currently carry under any name: high-skilled workers overestimate their expertise, consider GenAI unsuitable for their work, and disregard it — paradoxically marginalizing themselves in GenAI-augmented environments because their identity-defining skill sets misalign with new GenAI-collaboration skills. Distinct from [[novice-vulnerability]] (which is about juniors using AI too much) and [[upskilling-deskilling-paradox]] (which is about skill-level dynamics under AI use). This is genuinely new vocabulary; warrants a NEW concept entry.

The paper's productivity inventory in Table 2 is also a useful single-source roll-up of effect sizes from Brynjolfsson et al. (2025), Noy & Zhang (2023), Dell'Acqua et al. (2023), Toner-Rodgers (2024), Otis et al. (2024), Doshi & Hauser (2024), and others — all primary papers worth ingesting separately, but useful here as a citable summary table.

## Contradicts / Extends

- Extends [[professional-identity-threat]] — adds the BPNT three-needs decomposition; identity-threat is one expression of need-frustration rather than a free-standing construct. Adds the five-strategy coping vocabulary as a structured response taxonomy.
- Extends [[agency]] — "algorithmic cage" framing for mandatory integration; surveillance-and-monitoring as the most serious autonomy-related psychological threat; psychological-ownership erosion when workers perceive themselves as editors.
- Extends [[novice-vulnerability]] — locates novice-vulnerability in BPNT terms: AI satisfies competence (via productivity gain) and frustrates competence (via skill-development bypass) simultaneously, and the unstable equilibrium tilts toward frustration when external scaffolding is removed.
- Aligns with [[singh-yadav-competency-paradox-2026]] — Singh Yadav's "augmentation illusion" / Dreyfus-stage analysis is the developmental-trajectory specification of what Hermann et al. describe at the snapshot level.
- Aligns with [[passalacqua-less-ai-2024]] — review's claim that "Working with less AI makes perfect" (their phrasing of the Passalacqua finding) supports the BPNT-frustration framing of unrestricted AI use.

## Open Questions

- The paper explicitly closes with **outstanding questions** [pp.8-9] — the most KB-relevant: (a) which variables predict how workers choose compensatory strategies in response to which psychological threats? (perceived control, threat intensity); (b) how do workers respond to GenAI when it becomes a human-like coworker rather than a tool? (c) what factors contribute to resistance vs. acceptance? (d) what are the long-term effects of GenAI integration on workers and the nature of work? These are research-agenda items rather than KB gaps, but they define the boundary of what the BPNT framing currently can and cannot predict.
- The five-strategy taxonomy is imported from self-discrepancy theory (citing Stinson et al. and others) and applied analogically. The paper does not provide GenAI-specific empirical evidence for any of the five strategies operating in workplace contexts — they are predictions, not demonstrations. The KB should treat the taxonomy as a framework with strong theoretical grounding but limited direct empirical support in the GenAI context.
- The paper's productivity table inventories effects with effect sizes but does not adjudicate publication-bias concerns (Bartoš et al. 2026 found field-level meta-meta evidence that AI/learning effects collapse ~3× under bias correction). The same correction may apply to AI-at-work productivity claims; the table should be read as the unadjusted distribution, not the bias-adjusted truth.
- The "paradox of expertise" claim rests on a small set of studies (Schmitt et al. 2024; SimanTov-Nachlieli 2025; Bankins et al. 2024). Whether the pattern generalizes across professions and how it interacts with [[novice-vulnerability]] (does the field have a "U-shape" of AI utility — strong for juniors, weak for mid, strong for seniors who finally adopt?) is open.
- Hermann et al. do not engage with the [[performance-paradox]] explicitly (no Bastani / Fan citations) — the framing of "task outsourcing without mastery" is presented as a competence-frustration mechanism but not connected to the durable-learning literature. KB synthesis here: the review's competence-frustration pathway and the performance-paradox cite the same mechanism from different angles (psychological-needs vs. learning-science).
