# Mollick (2026) — Management as AI Superpower

## Citation

Mollick, E. (2026). Management as AI superpower: Thriving in a world of agentic AI. *One Useful Thing*, January 27, 2026.

**URL:** [oneusefulthing.org/p/management-as-ai-superpower](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/management-as-ai-superpower)

## Type

Article (Substack post, practitioner-academic; Mollick is Wharton)

## Key Insight

After running an experimental Wharton MBA class where executive students built startup prototypes in four days using Claude Code + ChatGPT/Claude/Gemini, Mollick observes that **the bottleneck isn't AI fluency — it's management**. Students with industry experience (doctors, managers, leaders) outperformed AI-native students because they knew how to scope problems, define deliverables, and recognize when output was off.

Mollick proposes the **Equation of Agentic Work**: deciding when to delegate to AI depends on three variables —

1. **Human Baseline Time** — how long the task takes you alone
2. **Probability of Success** — likelihood the AI produces an acceptable output on a given attempt
3. **AI Process Time** — request + wait + evaluate per attempt

Delegate to AI when Human Baseline Time × Probability of Success > AI Process Time × Expected Attempts. The arithmetic flips with task length: a 1-hour task with 30-minute checks pays off only at very high success rates; a 10-hour task pays off at much lower success rates because the expected savings dominate.

Three management levers improve the equation: **better instructions** (clear goals → higher P(success)), **better evaluation** (faster, more accurate assessment of AI output), and **easier evaluation criteria** (knowing what "done" looks like). All three are improved by **subject matter expertise** — domain knowledge is what lets you write good instructions, see when something's wrong, and correct it efficiently.

The structural reframe: AI changes management's underlying assumption. Traditional management assumes *talent is scarce and expensive*; you delegate because you can't do everything. AI inverts this: **talent is abundant and cheap; what's scarce is knowing what to ask for**. The skills dismissed as "soft" — scoping, defining deliverables, recognizing when output is off — turn out to be the hard ones.

For human thinking with AI: this is the practitioner-side counterpart to the KB's preservation argument. Where [[scan]], [[think-first]], and [[strategic-alternation]] argue from cognitive-science principles, Mollick argues from management-school principles. Both arrive at the same conclusion: **the human's contribution is judgment, scope, and evaluation, not execution**. That contribution is precisely what AI cannot supply for itself.

## Relevance

A practitioner formalization of the KB's preservation thesis from a different angle. Three contributions:

- **Decision rule.** The Equation of Agentic Work is a usable arithmetic for "should I delegate this to AI?" — concrete, contextual, actionable. Practitioners can apply it without invoking cognitive-science vocabulary. It complements [[scan]] (which is a knowledge-based rather than cost-based decision rule).
- **Validation that domain expertise is the lever.** Mollick's MBA observation aligns with [[bo-sycophancy-novices-2026]] (novices are most vulnerable to AI mistakes), [[hu-metamemory-offloading-2019]] (metacognitive overconfidence), and [[risko-gilbert-cognitive-offloading-2016]] (fluency-feeling masks evaluation failures). Domain expertise is what supplies the missing metacognitive checks.
- **Reinforces the asymmetry.** AI commoditizes execution; humans retain scoping, judgment, evaluation. This maps to the KB's distinction between [[execution-commoditization]] and the irreducibly-human work of judgment.

## Open Questions

- The Equation of Agentic Work assumes the human knows enough to evaluate. What happens when a junior worker delegates to AI on tasks where they lack the evaluation capacity? Mollick's MBAs had domain expertise; entry-level workers may not.
- Mollick's framing sells management skill as protective. But [[nikolova-robots-meaning-2024]] and [[passalacqua-less-ai-2024]] suggest the *meaning* of work suffers when humans become managers of AI agents rather than producers. Is "everyone is a manager" actually a desirable end state psychologically?
- The empirical claim is one MBA cohort over four days. This generalizes to short-form startup ideation — does it generalize to long-form deep work, regulatory submissions, surgical decision-making? Probably not uniformly.
