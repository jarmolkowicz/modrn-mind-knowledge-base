# The AI Disclosure Penalty

## Citation

Raj, M., Berg, J. M., & Seamans, R. (2026). The artificial intelligence disclosure penalty: Humans persistently devalue AI-generated creative writing. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*. Advance online publication. https://doi.org/10.1037/xge0001889

## Type

Paper

## Key Insight

Across 16 preregistered experiments (N = 27,491), disclosing AI involvement in creative writing consistently reduced evaluations by ~6.2% on average (Cohen's d = 0.24). The penalty is mediated by perceived authenticity and proved stubbornly resistant to multiple mitigation interventions derived from prior research.

## Relevance

Provides the largest and most rigorous evidence base for the [[disclosure-penalty]] to date. The sheer scale (16 experiments, 27K+ participants), the systematic testing and failure of multiple mitigation strategies, and the authenticity mechanism all strengthen the KB's treatment of this phenomenon. The finding that the penalty persists across different content types, evaluation metrics, and time periods (March 2023 to June 2024) makes this a cornerstone source.

## Key Findings

- **14 of 16 studies** showed significant negative AI disclosure effects (p < .05)
- **Mediation**: Perceived authenticity consistently mediated the penalty — AI-labeled content was seen as less authentic regardless of identical quality
- **Resistant to mitigation**: Framing as human-AI collaboration, humanizing the AI, increasing perceived AI capability, and putting a human "in the loop" all failed to reliably reduce the penalty
- **Cochran's Q test** (p = .46) confirmed effect consistency across studies — not driven by outlier experiments
- **Temporal stability**: No meaningful shift across 15 months despite rapid public exposure to generative AI
