# Illusion of Objectivity

## What It Is

The mistaken belief that AI tools either have no standpoint or are capable of representing all possible standpoints, when in fact they embed the standpoints of their training data and developers. Coined by Messeri & Crockett (2024) for AI in scientific research: AI Oracles and Arbiters are imagined as eliminating subjectivity from literature search and peer review; AI Surrogates are imagined as representing the full breadth of humanity in synthetic participants. Both framings are versions of the same illusion — one that "reasserts the fantasy of a single kind of knower masked as neutral and universal" (Messeri & Crockett 2024, p.7).

## Why It Matters

A perceived absence of standpoint is a powerful credibility signal. People extend more epistemic trust to sources they read as objective, and AI tools are described — in marketing, in research papers, in everyday talk — in ways that suggest a "view from nowhere." When this framing is internalized, three things happen:

1. **Embedded biases in AI outputs become harder to see** because users are not looking for them. Standpoints that were embedded by training data, labelling decisions, and developer judgements pass through as "what the tool says."
2. **Standpoint diversity is treated as redundant.** If AI represents everyone, why staff a project with a demographically or cognitively diverse team?
3. **"Strong objectivity" regresses to "weak objectivity."** Decades of work in philosophy of science (Harding 1995; Messeri & Crockett 2024 ref 157) distinguished *strong objectivity* (which accounts for the embodied standpoints of researchers and embraces standpoint diversity as a source of robustness) from *weak objectivity* (which fails to recognize standpoints exist at all). AI's framing as standpoint-free re-introduces weak objectivity wholesale.

## Key Insight

Objectivity is not the absence of standpoint. It is the *practice* of recognizing, accounting for, and triangulating across standpoints. AI tools cannot be objective in the strong sense — they are produced under specific social, economic, and disciplinary conditions, and their outputs reflect those conditions. Treating them as objective forfeits the practice that produces actual epistemic robustness.

Messeri & Crockett (2024, p.7) point to a historical pattern: the demographic homogeneity of mid-twentieth-century science was invisible from inside the field. Only after the field diversified did scientists notice that the prior monoculture had been embedding particular standpoints in claims that read as universal. AI risks recreating this dynamic at speed — re-homogenizing the ecosystem just as it had begun to diversify, while presenting the homogenization as neutrality.

## Where It Shows Up Outside Science

[Inference] The same illusion operates wherever AI tools are positioned as bias-removers in human judgement: hiring filters, performance reviews, content moderation, clinical decision support, peer review of any kind. The pattern is consistent: a tool produced under specific conditions is presented as a corrective to human bias, and its embedded biases become harder to challenge precisely because of that framing.
