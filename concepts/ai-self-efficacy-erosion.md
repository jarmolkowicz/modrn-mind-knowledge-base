# AI Self-Efficacy Erosion

## What It Is

Interacting with AI systems in evaluative contexts (e.g., AI-driven recruitment) reduces self-efficacy beliefs, increases self-objectification, and diminishes belief in free will. The mechanism: being evaluated by an algorithm makes people feel like data rather than agents, triggering a chain from reduced self-efficacy to self-objectification to diminished free will beliefs.

## Why It Matters

Self-efficacy is not just confidence — it is the belief that you can organize and execute actions to achieve goals. When AI interactions erode this belief, people don't just feel less capable; they begin to see themselves as objects (data points, resources) rather than agents. This is a distinct erosion pathway from [[cognitive-debt]] or [[confidence-competence-gap]] — it attacks not skill but self-perception.

## Key Insight

The causal chain is: AI interaction --> reduced self-efficacy --> increased self-objectification --> reduced belief in free will. This was consistent across three experiments (~571 participants), including one using a real AI recruiting system. The effect occurs even in short-term interactions — not just chronic exposure. Being processed by an algorithm, rather than evaluated by a human, triggers the objectification pathway.

## Evidence

- **Pilot + Study 1** (scenario-based): Participants imagining AI-evaluated job interviews reported lower self-efficacy and higher self-objectification than those imagining human interviews
- **Study 2** (real AI system): Participants interacting with an actual AI recruiting tool showed the same pattern with stronger ecological validity
- **Mediation**: Self-efficacy reduction mediated the relationship between AI interaction and self-objectification; self-objectification mediated the path to reduced free will beliefs
- **Sample**: ~571 Italian participants across three studies
