# Illusion of Exploratory Breadth

## What It Is

The mistaken belief that you are exploring the full space of testable hypotheses, when in fact you are exploring only the narrower subspace of hypotheses your tools can test. Coined by Messeri & Crockett (2024) for the AI-in-science context: as researchers adopt AI for hypothesis generation and testing, the questions AI is good at evaluating come to feel like all the questions there are. Distracted by the volume of new findings AI produces, researchers fail to notice that the search space has narrowed (Messeri & Crockett 2024, p.6).

## Why It Matters

This illusion is the metacognitive shadow of a [[scientific-monoculture]] of knowing. The danger is not that AI gives wrong answers; it is that AI reframes the question space so that questions outside its reach become invisible. A field that produces more papers than ever can simultaneously be exploring less of its actual problem domain.

For consulting, research, and analysis work outside science the same dynamic applies. Quantitative, structured, generalizable questions — the kinds AI can support — proliferate. Questions that require attention to subjectivity, subtlety, nuance, qualitative context, or local detail get marginalized not because anyone decides they are unimportant, but because the tools available to handle them are slower, less prestigious, and less productive.

## Key Insight

The illusion is not a perceptual error about a single output. It is a structural error about the *shape* of the question space. You can be entirely correct about every finding AI produces and still be wrong about what you are studying — because the set of questions AI made tractable is being mistaken for the set of questions worth asking.

Messeri & Crockett (2024, p.6) name three concrete pathways:

1. **Quantification crowds out qualitative ways of knowing.** AI tools translate complex phenomena into numbers — portable, aggregatable, comparable, but stripped of contextual sensitivity and local detail. Research questions that resist quantification get marginalized.
2. **Researcher degrees of freedom become invisible.** Curating training sets and designing training regimes involves dozens or hundreds of judgements that can produce different conclusions from the same starting data. Once the AI tool is built, those choices "disappear behind the objective sheen of a quantitative model" [p.6].
3. **Prediction crowds out explanation.** When predictive accuracy is the measure of success, predictively powerful but explanatorily empty models proliferate. Aggregating across many such models makes systemic errors hard to detect.

## How To Surface It

[Inference] No empirical countermeasure has been validated in this exact framing. Plausible diagnostic moves, in the spirit of the paper's prescriptions:

- **Name the vision in play.** Use the [[ai-vision-taxonomy]] to identify whether you are using AI as Oracle, Surrogate, Quant, or Arbiter — each has a distinct shape of question it favours.
- **Pre-register the questions you are *not* asking.** Articulate which hypotheses are out of reach for the AI you are using, and why. Make the narrowing visible.
- **Keep at least one non-AI track running.** Qualitative interviews, ethnographic work, single-case study, hand-built quantitative model — anything that does not depend on the same toolchain. Use it to triangulate, not just to corroborate.
- **Audit the breadth, not the depth.** Periodically inventory your research questions and check whether the distribution has shifted toward AI-friendly forms.
