# Artificial Certainty

## What It Is

The illusion that complex future outcomes are definitively knowable, produced when AI generates representations that appear authoritative, concrete, and unambiguous despite depicting inherently uncertain phenomena. AI removes visible markers of uncertainty from its outputs, making approximations look like definitive descriptions.

## Why It Matters

Artificial certainty undermines expert authority and enables non-experts to feel they fully comprehend complex phenomena simply because they have access to authoritative-looking AI representations. When laypeople mistake AI-generated models for reality, they bypass the process experts whose role is to calibrate how much certainty a representation should convey. This erodes both institutional decision-making quality and the developmental path through which expertise is built.

## Key Insight

Leonardi & Leavell (2026) found that the critical variable is not the AI tool itself but how experts deploy it. In a comparative ethnography of two urban planning organizations using the same AI simulation tool:

- **Enhancement** (amplifying AI capabilities in presentations) led stakeholders to mistake representations for reality, undermining expert authority
- **Modulation** (tempering how AI outputs are presented) preserved the uncertainty necessary for informed decision-making

The distinction maps to "representations *of* the future" (claiming to show what will happen) versus "representations *for* the future" (tools to support deliberation about what might happen).

Messeri & Crockett (2024) extend artificial certainty to the level of an entire knowledge-production community. Where Leonardi & Leavell describe AI representations stripping uncertainty markers from organizational decision-making, Messeri & Crockett describe the same dynamic operating across a scientific field: AI Oracles, Surrogates, Quants, and Arbiters each produce outputs whose visible confidence outruns their actual epistemic warrant, and the cumulative effect is a [[scientific-monoculture]] in which AI-friendly methods and standpoints crowd out alternatives. The two papers describe nested levels of the same mechanism — organizational artificial certainty (Leonardi & Leavell) is one mode of community-level monoculture (Messeri & Crockett).
