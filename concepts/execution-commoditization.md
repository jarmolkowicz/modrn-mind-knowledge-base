# Execution Commoditization

## What It Is

As AI agents become capable of performing complex professional tasks, the ability to execute becomes abundant and cheap. The scarce resource shifts from doing the work to knowing what good work looks like — defining it, evaluating it, and redirecting it when it falls short.

## Why It Matters

This reframes what professionals need to preserve. If execution is commoditized, the skills that matter are the ones AI can't replicate: scoping ambiguous problems, recognizing quality, making contextual tradeoffs, and accepting accountability for outcomes. These are judgment skills, not technical skills — and they develop through the very hands-on experience that AI now bypasses.

## Key Insight

Mollick (2026) reports that MBA students built functional startup prototypes in four days using AI tools — not because they had AI expertise, but because they had domain knowledge and management instincts. This is a teaching observation from a facilitated classroom setting with experienced students, not a controlled study — but the pattern is instructive. They could:
- Scope problems clearly enough for AI to execute
- Recognize when AI output met quality standards
- Redirect when output missed the mark

Three levers for effective AI delegation, all rooted in domain expertise:
1. **Better instructions** — subject matter expertise enables clearer goals
2. **Improved evaluation** — experts recognize quality problems faster
3. **Faster quality assessment** — domain knowledge enables quick judgment

The implication: prompt engineering is a transitional skill. Domain expertise and judgment are permanent advantages.

## The Preservation Tension

If execution builds the expertise needed to evaluate execution, commoditizing it creates a development gap. Today's experts can leverage AI because they already have judgment. But how do tomorrow's experts build judgment if they never do the hands-on work?

Niederhoffer et al. (2025/2026) provide empirical evidence for what execution commoditization looks like at organizational scale: workslop. When execution is commoditized (anyone can produce polished output via AI), the gap between production and quality judgment becomes visible. The result: polished work that "masquerades as good work, but lacks the substance to meaningfully advance a given task."

Key data point: ~50% of workslop recipients view the sender as less capable, creative, and reliable afterward. This suggests a market mechanism is emerging — when execution is cheap, others can detect the absence of quality judgment, and the producer's professional reputation suffers. Commoditized execution without judgment is not just ineffective; it actively damages professional standing.

The survey also found a protective factor: workers with "competence and control over AI tools" are half as likely to create workslop. This aligns with the entry's core claim that domain expertise and judgment remain permanent advantages. The workers who can evaluate AI output are the ones producing value; the rest produce workslop.
