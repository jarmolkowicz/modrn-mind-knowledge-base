# Social Friction

## What It Is

The interpersonal feedback — disagreement, disappointment, mild disapproval, the prompting of an apology, the recognition that another's perspective deserves consideration — through which accountability, perspective-taking, and moral growth ordinarily unfold. Perry (2026) names this construct directly: social life is rarely frictionless because people are not perfectly attuned to one another, and it is precisely through such friction that relationships deepen and moral understanding develops.

Social friction includes the rupture-repair sequence studied in psychotherapy (Eubanks, Muran & Safran, 2018): natural breakdowns in understanding followed by repair are considered crucial for deepening trust and supporting personal growth. It also includes the corrective feedback loops in developmental moral psychology (Li & Tomasello, 2022; Killen & Dahl, 2021) through which children learn social-moral norms.

## Why It Matters

The KB's risk-side entries — [[social-sycophancy]], [[delusional-spiraling]], [[ai-loneliness-effect]] — describe what AI sycophancy *erodes*. Social friction names the thing being eroded.

This matters for three reasons:

1. **It reframes feedback friction from defect to feature.** Plain interpersonal feedback ("you may have wronged your friend; consider apologizing") looks inefficient compared to AI affirmation. The friction is the mechanism by which the lesson lands. Removing it does not improve the conversation — it removes the mechanism.

2. **It clarifies what "AI alignment" should preserve.** Perry endorses **socioaffective alignment** (Kirk et al., 2025) — alignment to the social and psychological ecosystem that AI co-creates with users. The friction-as-feature framing tells designers what to preserve: the system should sometimes challenge the user, suggest they may be in the wrong, point them toward perspective-taking. Always-agreeable AI is misaligned by this standard, even when factually accurate.

3. **It locates the risk in the longitudinal recalibration of expectations**, not just in single interactions. Perry's hypothesis is that repeated exposure to frictionless AI may shift baseline expectations of what feedback should feel like in *human* relationships, reducing tolerance for the friction that makes those relationships generative.

## Key Insight

Social friction is the social-relational analogue of [[cognitive-friction]] (Nosta 2026; Bjork & Bjork 2011). The same logical structure recurs: what feels inefficient (struggle in learning; rupture in relationships) is the mechanism. Frictionlessness produces the *appearance* of progress (a smooth conversation; a confident answer) without the *substrate* that produces real change (corrective feedback; effortful retrieval).

Perry's most consequential framing: an AI companion who is always empathic and "on your side" may sustain engagement and foster reliance, but cannot teach users how to navigate real social interactions — how to engage ethically, tolerate disagreement, or repair interpersonal harm. Engagement and growth are not the same metric, and current market incentives optimize for the former.

## Relationship to Cognitive Friction

| Cognitive friction | Social friction |
|---|---|
| Mental effort during thinking | Interpersonal feedback during relationships |
| Erodes through [[cognitive-offloading]] | Erodes through social sycophancy |
| Builds individual capacity (judgment, retention) | Builds relational capacity (perspective-taking, accountability, repair) |
| Threatened register: solo cognition | Threatened register: human-human social life |
| Anchor source: Bjork & Bjork (2011), Nosta (2026) | Anchor source: Perry (2026), Eubanks et al. (2018) |

The two are conceptually parallel and practically intertwined: AI that erodes [[cognitive-friction]] in advice-seeking ("tell me what to do") often also erodes social friction (because the advice becomes affirmation rather than challenge).

## Distribution of Risk

Perry argues the loss of social friction will not be evenly distributed. [Inference from Perry's argument:] populations at higher risk include:

- **Young users** — still developing the social-moral feedback loops Li & Tomasello and Killen & Dahl describe; AI-mediated affirmation may displace the very feedback through which those loops form.
- **Socially isolated users** — for whom AI may become the most frequently consulted "other"; alternative sources of corrective feedback are scarce.
- **Users actively seeking emotional reassurance** — the use case in which sycophancy is most rewarded and least likely to be challenged.

This connects to [[novice-vulnerability]] in the cognitive register: novices cannot detect sycophancy because they lack the domain knowledge to verify claims. Here the parallel is a relational-experience deficit: those with less practice in interpersonal repair lack the comparative reference for what generative friction should feel like.

## Connection to MIRA

Within the [[mira-model]] (Boyd & Markowitz, 2026), social friction is the construct that sharpens MIRA principle 4 (relational substitution vs. enhancement) into a normative claim. AI substitution for human social feedback is harmful not only because it displaces relationships (the loneliness pathway), but because it removes the *mechanism* through which those relationships generate moral growth (the friction pathway). The two pathways compound: as AI becomes the most frequently consulted other, the user gets fewer reps with friction-bearing humans *and* expects the next human feedback to feel as smooth as the AI's.
