# Illusion of Explanatory Depth

## What It Is

The tendency to overestimate how well you understand how things work. People consistently report high confidence in their explanatory understanding of everyday phenomena (zippers, flush toilets, bicycle gears) — and that confidence drops sharply when they are asked to actually produce the explanation step by step. The illusion is specifically about *explanatory* knowledge, not facts, procedures, or narratives. It is more pronounced for explanations than for other knowledge types.

## Why It Matters

Explanatory understanding is what scientific (and professional) knowledge is largely *for*. If you mistakenly believe you understand the mechanism behind a phenomenon, you make worse decisions, generate worse predictions, and miss the questions you would otherwise have asked. AI use compounds this: AI tools can produce fluent, confident, mechanism-shaped outputs about almost any topic, and people who consult them tend to import the AI's apparent depth as their own.

Messeri & Crockett (2024) argue this illusion is the central metacognitive risk of AI in scientific research. When a scientist uses an AI Quant to model a phenomenon X and gets a high-accuracy prediction, the temptation is to conclude that X is now understood. The authors call this the **prediction–explanation fallacy** [p.4]: an accurate predictive model can bear little relation to the actual data-generating process. Predictive accuracy and explanatory understanding are different things, and AI systems are typically optimized for the former while being read as if they delivered the latter.

The risk is sharpest when the AI is used outside the user's domain of expertise — exactly the setting where AI is most appealing (it expands what you can do) and where users have the least basis to detect when an AI's output is too good to be true.

## Key Insight

Feelings of understanding and actual understanding are not always correlated. Reductive, quantitative, fluent, simple-feeling explanations *especially* trigger the feeling without delivering the substance — and AI is built to produce exactly those qualities (Messeri & Crockett 2024, p.4).

Diagnostic test (adapted from Rozenblit & Keil's procedure): rate your understanding of a phenomenon on a 1–7 scale. Now write or speak a step-by-step mechanistic explanation. Re-rate. The drop is the illusion.

## How AI Specifically Inflates It

1. **Fluent outputs feel like understood phenomena.** AI's grammatical, confident, mechanism-shaped language signals depth even when the underlying model is shallow or incorrect.
2. **Prediction is mistaken for explanation.** A predictive AI model that classifies X with 97% accuracy is read as a model that *explains* X. The two are different, and the gap can be enormous (Messeri & Crockett 2024, refs 112–115).
3. **Outside-domain use compounds the illusion.** Users who lack the expertise to spot errors are simultaneously more trusting of AI (Messeri & Crockett 2024, ref 116) and less able to verify what the AI claims.
4. **Aggregating across models hides the illusion at scale.** When many AI models are used and reported across a literature, errors are harder to localize and easier to inherit.
