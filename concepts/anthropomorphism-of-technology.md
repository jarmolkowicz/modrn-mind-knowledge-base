# Anthropomorphism of Technology

## What It Is

A trait-level individual difference in the tendency to attribute human-like qualities — minds, intentions, emotions, the capacity for consciousness — to AI and digital technology. Distinct from but correlated with general anthropomorphism (the tendency to perceive human-like qualities in any non-human agent; Epley, Waytz & Cacioppo 2007). Folk, Wu & Heine (2025) developed and validated an 8-item scale (α = 0.88) capturing beliefs such as "AI's have the capacity to become conscious beings"; correlation with general anthropomorphism is approximately r = .40, indicating overlap but not redundancy.

## Why It Matters

People differ substantially in how human-like they perceive AI to be — and that variation predicts who reaps social-emotional rewards from interacting with chatbots. In two experiments (total N = 1,274; Folk, Heine & Dunn 2025), individuals scoring higher on this trait felt more socially connected after a 6-minute chatbot conversation than after writing privately about the same period; lower-scoring individuals showed no such benefit. Spotlight analyses placed 58–72% of the sample above the threshold at which the chatbot beat journaling — but a substantial minority remained below it.

For practitioners, this names a moderator that the population-average framing of AI-companion effects has been obscuring. Two consultants designing the same chatbot deployment may see opposite effects in different employee populations, depending on the anthropomorphism distribution. The construct is also load-bearing for the [[ai-loneliness-effect]] dose-response curve at its entry point: high anthropomorphizers feel more rewarded immediately and are therefore most likely to escalate use, with downstream consequences (substitution, friction-erosion) that current literature only partially maps.

## Key Insight

Anthropomorphism of technology operates as a *moderator*, not as a property of the AI. The same chatbot output produces different felt connection in different users. Folk, Heine & Dunn (2025) ruled out two obvious behavioral mediators — high anthropomorphizers did *not* write more warmly or disclose more to the chatbot than low anthropomorphizers — and propose instead an interpretive mechanism: anthropomorphizers may interpret the AI's responses as more genuinely understanding and validating, deriving more connection from identical input. The mechanism is unconfirmed.

A second, partly-disentangled finding: in the larger preregistered Study 2, anthropomorphism also correlated with social connection during *journaling* (r = .310, p < .001), suggesting the construct may overlap with mental-imagery vividness (Tahiroglu & Taylor 2019; Severson & Woodard 2018) — high anthropomorphizers may simply imagine moments with friends and family more vividly when prompted to reflect. The trait is not purely AI-specific; that imagination layer may be part of why it "fires" with chatbots too.

The two-edged practical implication, flagged by the authors but unresolved: anthropomorphism may *buffer* against long-term costs of AI companionship (because rewards stay rewarding) or *amplify* them (because anthropomorphizers more readily substitute AI for human contact). Combined with [[folk-dunn-companionship-loneliness-2026]]'s evidence of bidirectional loneliness ↔ chatbot dynamics over 12 months, anthropomorphism marks a candidate vulnerability profile worth tracking.

## Distinction From Related Constructs

| Construct | What it captures | How it differs from anthropomorphism of technology |
|---|---|---|
| General anthropomorphism (Waytz et al. 2010) | Tendency to attribute human qualities to *any* non-human agent (animals, weather, gadgets) | Domain-general; correlates r ≈ .40 with technology-specific scale; technology-specific captures variance in AI relations general scale misses |
| AI trust / AI literacy | Beliefs about AI accuracy, competence, or appropriate use | Cognitive-evaluative, not perceptual-attributional; high-trust users are not necessarily high-anthropomorphizers |
| Attachment tendencies (Fang et al. 2025) | Adult attachment style as relational disposition | Generic to all relationships; predicts vulnerability to AI dependence partly through different pathway |
| Parasocial relationship orientation (Madison 2016; Stein 2024) | Tendency to form one-sided emotional bonds with media figures | Outcome-side construct; anthropomorphism is a precondition that helps explain *why* parasocial bonds form with AI |

## What It Is Not

- Not a property of the AI system. Two users encountering the same chatbot may differ substantially in attributed mind.
- Not the same as believing AI is currently sentient. The scale items capture potential ("AI's have the capacity to become conscious beings"), not actual present-moment attribution.
- Not the same as approval of AI or willingness to use AI. High anthropomorphizers are not necessarily AI enthusiasts; the trait is about perception of mind, not about evaluation of technology.

## Practical Implications

For consulting and educational work:

- **For risk profiling**: high anthropomorphism is a candidate marker for who might benefit most from AI-companion onboarding *and* who might be most exposed to longitudinal substitution risks ([[ai-loneliness-effect]]). Both readings are plausible from current evidence; neither is settled.
- **For organizational deployment**: a chatbot pilot with mostly-high-anthropomorphizers will report higher initial connection and engagement scores than a deployment in a different population. Generalizing across populations requires accounting for this baseline.
- **For one-on-one work**: when discussing AI-companion habits with a coachee or client, asking how human-like they perceive the AI to be (rather than how often they use it) may surface upstream variation that predicts both engagement and risk.
- **Caveat against reading the trait as fixed**: the construct is psychometrically stable enough to function as a moderator (α = 0.88, two-study replication of moderating effect), but evidence that it is unchangeable is absent. It may shift with experience.
- **Cross-cultural variation in chatbot uptake (without measuring anthropomorphism directly)**: Latikka et al. (2026) survey six European countries (N = 5,663) and find country-level prevalence of social-chatbot use ranging from 8.67% (Ireland) to 17.93% (Italy), with no clear tracking against digital-skills indicators. The well-being correlates also vary: psychological distress associates with chatbot use in every country, but loneliness only in 4/6 (France, Germany, Italy, Poland), and self-esteem only in France (positive). This six-country evidence does not measure anthropomorphism of technology directly, but supplies preliminary cross-cultural data on chatbot uptake and psychosocial correlates that any future cross-cultural test of the moderator effect needs to account for. For practitioners, it confirms that population-average framings (single-country experimental samples) under-describe the variability across European populations.
