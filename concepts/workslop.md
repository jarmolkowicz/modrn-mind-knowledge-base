# Workslop

## What It Is
AI-generated work content that looks plausibly polished but lacks the substance to meaningfully advance a task. Unlike traditional sloppy work, workslop uniquely uses machines to offload cognitive work to another human being — the recipient must interpret, correct, or redo the work.

## Why It Matters
Workslop reframes AI risk as an organizational problem, not just an individual one. When fluent AI output is passed along without substantive human input, the burden shifts downstream. The cost isn't just wasted time — it erodes trust, damages collaboration, and creates a negative feedback loop where AI adoption undermines the relationships it depends on.

## Key Insight
From BetterUp Labs / Stanford Social Media Lab survey (N=1,150):
- 40% of employees received workslop in the last month
- 53% admit to sending it
- Each incident costs ~2 hours of recipient time
- ~50% of recipients view senders as less capable, creative, and reliable afterward
- 42% see senders as less trustworthy

Workslop is not laziness — it is what happens when overwhelmed employees are given vague mandates to "use AI" without training, agency, or trust. 53% of respondents admitted to sending subpar AI-generated work at least some of the time — primarily because they feel "stretched too thin."

## Mechanism
```
Vague AI mandate + Overburdened employees + No quality norms
    → Performative AI use (demonstrate compliance)
    → Polished but substance-lacking output
    → Recipient bears the cognitive burden
    → Trust erosion + Collaboration breakdown
    → Resistance to AI adoption
```

## What Protects Against It
- **Competence and control over AI tools** — halves workslop creation
- **Team trust** — reduces workslop by 61%
- **Agency + Optimism** ("Pilots" vs "Passengers") — high-agency workers use AI more but create better output
- **Clear norms** — "AI is a collaborative tool, not a shortcut"

## The Irony
"The greatest irony of all is that to make AI work at work, we need to get better at being human."
